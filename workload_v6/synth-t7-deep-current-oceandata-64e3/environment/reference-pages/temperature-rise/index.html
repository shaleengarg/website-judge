The Ocean
Has a Fever.

The August 2023 anomaly sat 4.6 standard deviations above the 1982–2011 daily mean — a deviation with a sub-1-in-a-million expectation under stationary climate assumptions. The climate is not stationary.

Where The Heat Lives

IAP v3

Surface temperature is only the top millimetre of the story. 91% of trapped greenhouse heat is absorbed into the ocean, accumulating downward through the upper 2000 metres. Each basin tells a different version of the same accumulation.

Heat absorbed since 1971
380 ZJ

Equivalent to detonating 5 Hiroshima-scale energies per second, continuously, for 53 years.

Greenhouse heat into ocean
91%

Atmosphere absorbs 1%; land 5%; cryosphere 3%. The ocean carries nearly the entire energy imbalance.

Thermocline Shift, 1990 → 2024

ARGO + WOA

Warming concentrates near the surface, sharpening the boundary between the warm mixed layer and the cold deep ocean. A steeper thermocline suppresses vertical mixing, starves the surface of nutrients, and traps heat in shallower water.

The ocean is not warming uniformly — it is sharpening. A thinner mixed layer means more concentrated surface heat, more frequent marine heatwaves, and a thicker barrier between life-supporting nutrients below and photosynthesis above. — IAP Annual Report, 2024

Methods

SST anomalies derived from the ERSSTv5 reconstruction (NOAA NCEI) gridded at 2°×2°, with HadSST4 cross-validation. OHC integrated 0–2000 m using IAP/CAS gridded product with bias-corrected ARGO float profiles from 2005 onward, XBT-corrected prior. Thermocline analysis uses the World Ocean Atlas 2023 climatology and ARGO trajectories binned by basin.

Uncertainty

Pre-1950 SST records carry ±0.08°C uncertainty from sparse shipboard sampling. OHC uncertainty halves with ARGO deployment (2005). Decadal trends reported to 95% confidence. All anomalies relative to the 1901–2000 climatological mean unless otherwise noted.
