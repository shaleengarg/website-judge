The Four Wetland Types We Steward

From salt-soaked mangrove fringe to cypress-shadowed freshwater swamp, the Gulf Coast harbors a mosaic of tidal and transitional habitats. Brackwater Marsh Conservancy actively manages restoration plots across all four of these ecosystem classes.

Type 01 · Saline Coastal

Black Mangrove Fringe

& &&&& &&|&&& &&|& ~~~~~|~~~~~

Salt-tolerant Avicennia germinans forms the Gulf's northernmost mangrove community, anchoring shoreline sediments with pneumatophores—pencil-like aerial roots that pierce the mudflat. These fringe forests buffer storm surge, filter nitrogen runoff, and shelter juvenile redfish and snook. Cold snaps periodically prune them back; climate warming is extending their range northward each decade.

Range
South Texas to Cedar Key, FL
3,840
Acres protected
Type 02 · Saline Tidal

Spartina Salt Marsh

\|/ \|/ \|/ | | | ~~~~~~~~~~~~~ ~ ~ ~ ~

Smooth cordgrass (Spartina alterniflora) dominates the low intertidal zone, flooding twice daily and exporting detritus that fuels the entire Gulf food web. These marshes sequester carbon at rates rivaling tropical forests, anchor the shoreline against erosion, and serve as nursery grounds for blue crab, brown shrimp, and over seventy fish species critical to the region's commercial fisheries.

Range
Brownsville, TX to Tampa Bay, FL
6,210
Acres protected
Type 03 · Mixohaline

Brackish Transition Zone

* \|/ * \|/ | \|/ ~~~|~~~|~~~|~ ~ ~ . ~ . ~

Where freshwater rivers meet incoming tides, salinity fluctuates between 5 and 18 parts per thousand, creating mixed stands of bulrush, saltmeadow cordgrass, and black needlerush. These transitional bands are among the most productive yet vulnerable habitats on the coast, supporting diamondback terrapins, mottled ducks, and clapper rails while absorbing nutrient loads from upstream agriculture.

Range
Estuary mouths, TX–LA–MS
2,975
Acres protected
Type 04 · Freshwater Forested

Freshwater Cypress Swamp

/\ /\ /__\/__\ || || ~~~~||~~||~~~ /||\/||\

Bald cypress (Taxodium distichum) and tupelo gum tower over slow-moving freshwater backwaters just inland of the saline marshes. Centuries-old trees host wood ducks, prothonotary warblers, and basking alligators, while their buttressed trunks and knee roots slow floodwaters during hurricane events. Saltwater intrusion from sea-level rise is the swamp's most urgent existential threat.

Range
Coastal LA, AL & FL panhandle
1,175
Acres protected
Bioindicators

Six Species That Tell Us the Marsh is Working

Healthy estuarine systems reveal themselves through their inhabitants. The species below are monitored on a quarterly schedule by Conservancy field teams; population trends inform our restoration priorities and grant allocations.

Roseate Spoonbill

Platalea ajaja

This vivid pink wader sweeps shallow brackish ponds for shrimp and small fish, its specialized bill detecting prey by touch. Spoonbill nesting colonies are sensitive to disturbance and water-level shifts, making them a flagship indicator of estuary stability.

Diamondback Terrapin

Malaclemys terrapin

North America's only obligate brackish-water turtle, the terrapin grazes on periwinkle snails that would otherwise overconsume Spartina shoots. Its decline signals overharvest, crab-trap bycatch, or loss of sandy nesting beaches adjacent to the marsh.

Gulf Killifish

Fundulus grandis

A hardy, finger-sized fish that thrives across a wide salinity range and dominates marsh-edge minnow communities. Robust killifish populations indicate clean tidal exchange and intact vegetative cover, while sudden absences flag hypoxia or chemical contamination events.

Louisiana Iris

Iris giganticaerulea

This native blue-violet iris blooms in freshwater swamp margins and shallow ditches during early spring. Wild stands signal freshwater hydrology and intact soil chemistry; their disappearance often precedes saltwater intrusion into cypress systems.

Sea Ox-Eye Daisy

Borrichia frutescens

A silver-leaved, salt-tolerant perennial that colonizes the upper marsh berm just above tidal flooding. Dense ox-eye stands stabilize the marsh-upland boundary and provide critical nectar for native bees and migrating monarch butterflies.

American Alligator

Alligator mississippiensis

An ecosystem engineer whose "gator holes" retain freshwater through dry seasons and create microhabitat for fish, herons, and turtles. Stable alligator densities in cypress and brackish zones reflect adequate prey, water quality, and minimal human conflict.

Field data note — Indicator surveys are conducted in March, June, September, and December at 22 fixed monitoring stations across the four wetland types. Aggregated annual results are published in the Conservancy's State of the Marsh report each February.
