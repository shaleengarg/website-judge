Our Mission · Est. 2009

Restoring the Gulf's Living Edge

Founded in 2009 by coastal ecologist Dr. Renata Solís following the catastrophic erosion of the Nueces Bay shoreline, Brackwater Marsh Conservancy is a Gulf Coast nonprofit dedicated to restoring tidal wetlands and the communities that depend on them. We work where freshwater meets the sea — the brackish, breathing frontier of the continent.

Mission Statement

Why Tidal Wetlands Matter

The Pulse of the Estuary

Tidal wetlands are the most productive ecosystems on Earth, exchanging nutrients, sediment, and life between river and sea twice each day. Along the Gulf Coast, they shelter juvenile shrimp and redfish, anchor sediment against storm surge, and sequester carbon at rates that outpace tropical forests.

Yet for every acre we protect, another is lost to subsidence, salinization, or development. Brackwater Marsh Conservancy exists to reverse that math — one restored bayou, one replanted Spartina plug, one community partnership at a time.

The Rising Threat

The Texas and Louisiana coasts are subsiding while seas rise — a double squeeze that pushes salt water into freshwater swamps and drowns marshes faster than they can accrete. Hurricanes Harvey, Ida, and Beryl each rewrote the shoreline in ways that took decades to occur naturally.

Our restoration work is calibrated to this acceleration. We plant for the marsh of 2055, not 1985, building elevation gradients and freshwater refugia that give wildlife room to migrate upslope as the Gulf encroaches.

The Three-Pillar Strategy

Brackwater's work rests on three interlocking pillars. Each reinforces the others: restored marsh becomes a living classroom; educated citizens become informed advocates; advocacy unlocks the public funding and policy that scales restoration.

We refuse to treat any single pillar as sufficient. A planted shoreline without community ownership erodes. A trained student without habitat to study disengages. A policy without science behind it collapses.

Restoration

Replanting native grasses, removing invasives, and reopening tidal flow on the ground, parcel by parcel.

Education

K–12 curriculum, field labs, and adult workshops that turn estuary literacy into civic action.

Advocacy

Coalition work with municipal, state, and tribal partners to defend wetland law and funding.
