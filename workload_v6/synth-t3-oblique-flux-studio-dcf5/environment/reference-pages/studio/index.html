03 — Studio

A practice at the seam between institutions, ideas, and audiences.

Oblique Flux is a thirteen-person studio working across brand systems, editorial, environmental graphics, and digital product for museums, research labs, foundations, and cultural agencies on three continents.

We founded Oblique Flux in 2011 to make complex public-interest work legible — and to give scientific and cultural institutions a design language as rigorous as the ideas they hold.

Philosophy04 sections
01 · Posture

Clinical, but never cold.

We treat each commission as a research problem first and a design problem second. That means long reading lists, stakeholder interviews that don't stop at the marketing team, and an insistence that the work answer to evidence rather than taste. The output is warm because the underlying clarity is real — not because we've decorated it to feel that way.

02 · Scope

One studio, one team, one signature.

We don't run satellite production shops or hand strategy to one office and design to another. Every engagement is delivered by a single integrated team of three to six people, drawn from across our three offices, with the original strategists staying through launch. Continuity is how good thinking survives the contact with reality.

03 · Method

Make the language before you make the thing.

Before any pixel or sample is produced, we work with clients to articulate the vocabulary of the project — what its principles are, who it is for, what it refuses to do. This shared language is the most durable artefact we leave behind, and it lets institutions make confident decisions long after the engagement closes.

04 · Ethic

Public-interest work, on public-interest terms.

The majority of our clients are museums, research institutes, universities, and civic-tech non-profits. We publish our methods, release toolkits openly where we can, and reserve a fifth of our annual capacity for partners that cannot pay our standard rates. The discipline of design is most useful when it widens the circle of who gets to participate.

House note

We sign work that we'd be willing to defend in a public seminar. If a brief or a stakeholder asks us to obscure rather than clarify, we say so early — and, occasionally, we walk away.
