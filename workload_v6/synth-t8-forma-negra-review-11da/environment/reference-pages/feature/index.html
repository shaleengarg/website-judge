Against Smooth Surfaces: Texture as Argument in Post-Digital Graphic Design

A reckoning with the seamless, the gradient, and the rendered — and an argument that the next graphic vocabulary will be one that resists the swipe, the polish, and the algorithmic mean.

ByInes Marquard
PhotographyStudio Halbschatten
FiledMarch 2024 · Berlin
IssueVol. 12 · No. 02

The promise of post-digital graphic design was supposed to be liberation: a return to material thinking after two decades of pixel-flat orthodoxy, a re-engagement with the hand, the press, the photocopier, the misregister. What we received instead was a vocabulary of texture as decoration — grain layered onto vector, noise applied as filter, the rusticity of imperfection reduced to a checkbox in a panel labeled "Effects." This essay is an argument for texture as argument: not as styling, but as the load-bearing structure of a graphic claim.

Consider, briefly, the genealogy. The Swiss grid was never neutral; its smoothness was a politics. Wim Crouwel's New Alphabet, often misread today as a celebration of the machine, was in fact a polemic about the violence of constraint — a typeface that hurt to read because it had to.

By the late 1990s, the dominant rhetoric of digital interface design had collapsed all friction into the singular category of "bad UX." This was, and remains, a category error. Friction is not the opposite of usability; friction is the texture of attention.

§ 01 — The Surface Question

Walk through any contemporary design biennial and you will encounter the same palette of textures used to signal authenticity: risograph bleed, halftone overlay, photocopy ghost, paper grain. These signs are not neutral. They are deployed precisely because the underlying work is, in every material sense, frictionless — vector files, screen-rendered, never touched.

The texture, in such cases, performs a kind of forensic alibi. It testifies to a labor that did not happen. It is the graphic equivalent of distressed denim: a manufactured indexical sign of wear that has been engineered to bypass actual wear.

"Smooth surfaces lie about the conditions of their making. Rough surfaces are not honest by default — but they at least permit the question to be asked." — I.M., on the politics of finish

The critical question is not whether a designer uses noise, grain, or grit, but whether the texture indexes a real material decision. Did the surface emerge from the process, or was it applied to it? This is the central distinction the post-digital discourse has refused to make, because making it implicates almost every studio working today.

A short taxonomy of false friction

I propose three categories. The first: cosmetic friction, in which texture is layered on as the final pass — the noise filter, the grain overlay, the simulated misregistration. The second: nostalgic friction, in which the visual codes of an earlier production technology are reproduced wholesale, regardless of whether the underlying object justifies them. The third, and rarest: structural friction, in which the texture is a consequence of the production system itself.

Only the third has anything to say. The first two are quotations of arguments that other designers, in other decades, were entitled to make.

§ 02 — Materiality as Method

Among the studios I surveyed — forty-seven of them, distributed across Berlin, Mexico City, Seoul, and Lisbon — only a small minority described their relationship to texture as methodological. The majority described it as aesthetic. This distinction, while it may seem semantic, is the entire essay.

A methodological relationship to texture means that the surface of the work is determined by the constraints and possibilities of its production. A risograph print looks the way it does because of the drum, the ink viscosity, the registration tolerance. To reproduce that look in CMYK offset is not homage; it is theft of an argument the original technology was making about color, opacity, and labor.

The studios working methodologically with texture are, almost without exception, the ones still operating their own production equipment. Hato Press in London. Tan & Loose in Chicago. Idea Books' in-house bindery. These are not nostalgic operations; they are studios whose graphic vocabulary is constrained, in productive ways, by what their machines can and cannot do.

The argument from constraint

Constraint, in the post-digital condition, is not a given. It is a choice. To work within constraint today is to refuse the infinite resolution and infinite palette of the screen, and to accept — politically, not merely aesthetically — the limits of a particular material situation.

This refusal is the only available foundation for a graphic claim that is not immediately recuperable as content. Every other gesture has been absorbed.

§ 03 — The Algorithmic Mean

There is a second, more urgent force pressing on the surface: the algorithmic mean. Generative tools trained on the entire indexed corpus of contemporary graphic design produce, by their nature, the median output of that corpus. The texture they generate is the average of every texture they have ingested — a kind of texture-flavored smoothness, indistinguishable in its sameness.

This is not a technology problem. It is a problem of what the texture is for. If the texture is for signaling — for marking the work as belonging to a current taste — then the algorithmic mean serves that function perfectly. If the texture is for arguing, then the algorithmic mean is its negation.

The studios I spoke to who were most resistant to generative tools were not resistant on craft grounds. They were resistant because they understood, correctly, that the argument a piece of design makes is partly an argument against the median. To accept the median as your starting surface is to forfeit the argument before it begins.

Three propositions

First: that texture is not a finish but a foundation. The graphic argument begins with the substrate, not with the typography. Second: that the choice of constraint is the most political decision a designer makes — more political than the choice of subject, more political than the choice of typeface. Third: that the post-digital is not behind us; it has not yet begun. What we have lived through is the digital's long and exhausting last gasp.

"To work within constraint today is to refuse the infinite resolution of the screen, and to accept the limits of a particular material situation." — Marquard, § 02

The next graphic vocabulary will be one in which the surface is no longer decorative. It will be one in which the question "how was this made?" is not rhetorical but answerable, and the answer is legible in the work itself — not by simulation, but by inheritance.

Whether this vocabulary will emerge from the studios I have named, or from somewhere else entirely, is unanswerable. What is answerable is the question of where it will not come from. It will not come from the smooth.
