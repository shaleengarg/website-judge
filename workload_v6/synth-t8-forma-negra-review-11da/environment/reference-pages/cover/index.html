From the Editors PP. 008 — 011

The twelfth volume of Forma Negra Review opens, deliberately, on a rough surface. After six years arguing that print and screen are not opposed media but adjacent grammars, we found ourselves circling a quieter, more stubborn problem: what to do with the textures, snags, and refusals that neither system likes to acknowledge.

This issue calls that residue friction. Not as obstacle, not as nostalgia, but as evidence — the mark a system leaves when it argues with the world. The fourteen contributors assembled here have spent the year reading these marks closely, in offset plates and pedestrian signage, in municipal forms and the menus of departure halls.

Anika Vellman's cover essay sets the polemical pitch: that the contemporary preference for smooth surfaces is not a neutral aesthetic but a policy, with measurable consequences for whose languages persist and whose typographies are quietly retired. Hugo Pareja answers her from the street, walking the unsigned edges of three South American capitals.

Elsewhere, Mira Östlund returns to the offset plate as a site of small, productive failures; Devon Asare audits the default settings of nine well-funded studios; and Cecile Marot reopens the question of why three twentieth-century typefaces — Plantin, Akzidenz-Grotesk, Eurostile — refuse to be metabolised by the present.

We have tried, in the make-up of this volume, to honour the argument. The grid is denser than usual. The rules are heavier. The captions are longer. Read at the speed of paper.

— The Editors, Rotterdam · September 2024

“Friction is not the absence of design. It is the place where a system finally tells you what it actually believes.” — A. Vellman, p. 016
