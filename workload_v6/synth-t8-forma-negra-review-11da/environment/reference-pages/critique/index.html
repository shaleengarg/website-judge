Three Objects · Three Verdicts · One Hour
CRITIQUE

In which our editors test the resilience, legibility, and ethical posture of three recent design objects against a six-criterion rubric — and award no medals.

Object № 01 / Identity System

A Brutal Civic Identity for Helsinki Transit, Refined Past the Point of Friction

By Eira Lindqvist

The new HSL identity arrives in a uniform of cobalt and gravel — a system so disciplined it threatens to disappear. Where the 1998 mark squawked, the 2024 mark exhales. This is not a complaint, but it is a reservation: a city identity that refuses to misbehave at the kerb has surrendered an instrument.

Typographically the suite leans on a custom Grotesk with humanist outliers — a tail on the lowercase 'l', an opened 'a'. Wayfinding pictograms have been deboned of their 80s curves into something close to ISO neutrality. The signage prototypes I photographed last winter at Pasila held up at 40 metres; the same panels at 4 metres felt evacuated of voice.

What this system does best is endure. What it does worst is surprise. Helsinki is a city of small frictions — ice, language, light — and the identity should bear a few of them too.

Verdict: Excellent infrastructure. Diminished personality.
Object № 02 / Printed Matter

Riso Monograph, Self-Published, Bound in Failure

By Hugo Marçal

'Soft Tools', a 168-page Riso anthology from the Porto collective Mãoseca, sells its imperfections as ideology. The misregistration is genuine; the binding is genuinely bad. The question is whether genuineness is enough.

Spine cracks at spread 14. Ink set-off on 22. A loving rebellion against the laser-perfect monograph, yes — but rebellion without craft is just attrition. The strongest sections (an essay on Cape Verdean signage by Tavares) survive on the merit of their writing.

Verdict: Sincere, smudged, and structurally precarious.
Object № 03 / Digital Artefact

A Reading App That Mistakes Animation for Care

By Naledi Okafor

'Marginal', a long-form reading app from a Berlin studio, makes a wager that page turns should feel like silk. The pages comply; the reading does not. Every animation costs a beat, and beats compound into hours.

Underneath sits a respectable rendering engine and an excellent typeface licensing model. But the surface is busy convincing me it is calm.

Verdict: Beautiful, costly, and quietly exhausting.
“A criticism is not a verdict on whether an object pleases; it is an audit of whether the object can be defended in the room it claims to enter.” — Editorial, Forma Negra Review, Vol. 12

Footnotes & Apparatus

01. HSL identity refresh launched 02.2024. Studio: Tsto / Werklig. Total program cost: €1.4M over 38 months.

02. 'Soft Tools' ed. of 320, four Riso colours (federal blue, fluorescent pink, sunflower, black). Distributed Porto / Lisbon / Berlin.

03. Cf. Lupton, E. 'Thinking with Type', 2nd ed., 2010, pp. 41–48 on the ethics of imperfection in DIY printed matter.

04. Editor disagreement on Marginal's legibility score (4 / 7 / 6) resolved by motion path inspection on iPad Pro M2.

05. KADIST wayfinding measured against ISO 7001 contrast standards at 3.5m and 12m. Both passed.

06. Carrera Bookplate is a 64pp letterpress monograph by Atelier 17, São Paulo. Edition of 180, signed.

07. 'Liminal' typeface released 09.2023 by Future Fonts contributor M. Reyes. Currently at v0.4.

08. Praça Pavilion (Lisbon Triennale 2023) used reclaimed cork and CNC oak — full BoM published openly.

09. All production-ethics scores incorporate disclosed supply chain, fair labour audits, and material end-of-life pathways.

10. Bauhaus reissue refers to Lars Müller's 2024 facsimile of the 1925 Bauhausbücher series.

11. Zürich Ballot Form redesigned by Studio Atelier Kontrast for the 2023 cantonal elections. Reduced spoilage 7.2%.
