Field Notes

Tips & Swaps

Five small lessons learned over many braises, plus honest substitutions for when the pantry — or the guest list — won't quite cooperate.

Tip 01

Choosing the right shank weight

Aim for shanks around 380–420 g each, bone-in, with a generous cap of fat. Anything smaller dries out before the connective tissue surrenders; anything heftier won't fit four to a pot without crowding. Ask your butcher for the foreshank — it has more collagen than the hind.

Tip 02

Dried beans vs. canned

Dried cannellini, soaked overnight and simmered with a bay leaf, give the ragout its silky body and a starchy broth that thickens the sauce naturally. Canned will work in a pinch — rinse them well and add only in the last 30 minutes of braising so they don't disintegrate. You'll lose some velvet, but gain an hour.

Tip 03

If you don't have dry vermouth

Vermouth's herbal backbone is doing real work here. The closest swap is a crisp dry white — a Vermentino or unoaked Sauvignon Blanc — with a small splash of Lillet Blanc or a half teaspoon of dried thyme stirred in. Avoid sweet vermouth; it will turn the sauce cloying.

Tip 04

Make-ahead braising

This dish is better on day two. Braise the shanks fully, cool in the liquid, then refrigerate overnight in the pot. The next day, lift the solidified fat cap off, gently rewarm at 150 °C until the sauce loosens, then finish the ragout fresh. The flavour deepens; the meat firms just enough to slice cleanly.

Tip 05

Reheating without drying out

Never microwave a braise. Return shanks and sauce to a heavy pot, add a splash of stock or water, cover tightly, and warm over the lowest possible flame for 20–25 minutes, basting once. If the sauce has tightened too far, finish with a knob of cold butter off the heat to bring back the gloss.

Swaps

Honest substitutions, tested in the same pot.

Ingredient swaps

  • Lamb
    → Beef osso buco (cross-cut veal or beef shank); extend braise by 30 minutes.
  • Cannellini
    → Borlotti, butter beans, or tinned chickpeas (added late).
  • Anchovy
    → 1 tsp white miso, or 2 tsp soy + a pinch of salt for similar umami depth.
  • Rosemary
    → Fresh thyme and a single bay leaf; gentler but still resinous.

Dietary adaptations

  • Gluten-free
    Naturally GF as written — confirm your stock and vermouth are certified, and skip the flour dredge before searing.
  • Dairy-free
    The recipe contains no dairy; omit the optional butter finish and use a generous drizzle of good olive oil instead.
  • Low-sodium
    Use unsalted stock, halve the anchovy, and season the shanks lightly before searing — the reduction concentrates salt considerably.
