Cover Story · Atlantic Margins

The Last Peat Bogs of Connemara

On the bruised, breathing skin of western Ireland, a thousand generations of carbon are running out of room — and a community of bogwalkers, hydrologists, and turfcutters is trying, against the weather, to decide what to save.

Dispatch № 084 Reading time 22 min Filed from Roundstone, Co. Galway April 2024

Fig. 0 — A blanket bog at first light near Errisbeg, Connemara. The dark tarns are pools formed where peat has slumped after recent cutting. Composite illustration based on field sketches, March 2024.

By Síle Ní Bhraonáin, with reporting by Cormac Devlin Senior Field Editor · PhD Quaternary Ecology, NUI Galway · 14 years on the western seaboard

Before the rain returns, which it will, the bog at Roundstone makes a sound like a wet hand pressed slowly to a windowpane. It is the sound of fifteen thousand years exhaling at once — water leaving the upper crust of sphagnum, peat warming a half-degree above the mean, methane bubbling sideways through root tunnels of long-dead pine. Bríd Ó Conghaile, who has walked this ground three mornings a week since 1986, hears it before I do. "She's drying," she says, planting her stick. "She shouldn't be, not in March." The stick comes out of the moss black to the wrist. The hole it leaves, no wider than a coin, fills back in within seconds. That is the trick of a healthy bog. The trick, increasingly, that the bogs of Connemara are forgetting.

I · A Skin of Water

What the Mire Remembers

Blanket bog is the most reluctant landscape in Europe. It accretes at a rate of roughly one millimetre per year — slower than fingernails, faster than basalt — and only where rainfall exceeds 1,250 millimetres and the summers refuse to fully dry. Connemara, with its 220 wet days and its low Atlantic gloom, is one of the last places on the continent where the conditions still hold. Roughly 70 per cent of the world's surviving blanket bog lies within a 600-kilometre arc that includes western Ireland, the Outer Hebrides, and the flow country of Caithness. Within that arc, the Irish proportion has lost an estimated 82 per cent of its original area since 1814, when the first ordinance survey began counting.

The peat itself is a ledger. Pollen grains, beetle wings, charcoal flecks, the tiny silica skeletons of testate amoebae — each layer is a paragraph in a record that, in undisturbed cores, runs without interruption from the late Younger Dryas to last Tuesday. Hydrologists call this the catotelm, the saturated dark body of the bog. Above it lies the acrotelm, a living scalp of sphagnum and cottongrass perhaps fifteen centimetres deep, where almost all of the bog's ecological business is conducted.

"A bog is not a wetland that became a soil. It is a soil that refused to stop being water." — D. Brennan, Bog Hydrology Unit, UCD

That refusal — water staying water, refusing the slow downward gravity of becoming clay or sand or anything more decisive — is what stores the carbon. Globally, peatlands cover only 3 per cent of land surface but contain twice as much carbon as the world's standing forests combined. The Connemara bogs alone hold approximately 38 megatonnes of fixed carbon in their upper two metres, according to a 2022 inventory by Teagasc. Drain them, and that ledger begins to be read aloud, slowly, in CO₂.

II · Cutting Rights

Turbary, Treaty, Trespass

The right to cut turf — turbary, in the old legal Latin still preserved in deeds across the parish — is one of the oldest property entitlements in the Irish countryside, predating the Land Acts and surviving every reorganisation since. To a household in Carna or Cashel, turbary is not nostalgia. It is heat, and the proof of belonging to a place. To the European Commission's habitats directorate, however, turf-cutting on a designated Special Area of Conservation is, since 2010, an infringement attracting fines of up to €15,000 per hectare per year, levied not on the cutters but on the Irish state.

The collision has been quiet, but it has been a collision. Between 2011 and 2023, the National Parks and Wildlife Service paid out €34 million in turbary compensation, relocating cutters from 53 protected bogs to alternative sites — most of them already degraded, some of them no longer functioning peat systems at all. Of the roughly 4,200 households affected, fewer than half have accepted the offered swap. The rest cut on, quietly, in the dawn, when satellite passes are infrequent and the wardens are at home.

"I'm not a criminal," says Padraig Joyce, who has cut on the same bank above Lough Inagh for forty-one years. "I'm a man making the fire. They can call it what they want." Joyce is sixty-eight. His sons cut beside him. His grandson, fourteen, does not, and says he does not intend to. Whether this is the slow attrition the Commission expected, or simply the same demographic erosion that is closing every rural post office from Clifden to Leenaun, depends on whom you ask.

"We were asked to choose between the climate and our grandfathers. Nobody told us they were the same person." — P. Joyce, turfcutter, Lough Inagh
III · Hydrology Under Pressure

The Slow Bleed of Drains

What kills a bog, mostly, is not the cutting. It is the drains. Once the surrounding fields are ditched for sheep grazing or commercial forestry — almost universally Sitka spruce, planted in the state-subsidised waves of the 1960s and 1990s — the bog dome begins to subside at its margins. The water table drops by between four and eleven centimetres a year, depending on slope. Sphagnum, which needs to be wet to the touch, retreats. Heather, gorse, and bracken move in. Within two decades a living mire becomes a brown, fibrous mat, oxidising in the open air at roughly one tonne of CO₂ per hectare per year.

Doctor Aoife Mulkerrin, who runs the Connemara Bog Hydrology Project from a converted creamery in Recess, has been measuring twelve catchments since 2014. "Eight of them are losing water faster than the rainfall can replace it," she says. "Three are in equilibrium. One — only one — is gaining." That one, Cloonacartan, lies inside the boundary of a community-managed restoration trial begun in 2019, in which volunteers have hand-blocked 1,140 drains using locally cut sods. The cost: under €300 per hectare. The carbon avoided, conservatively: 2.4 tonnes per hectare per year. Compared with afforestation subsidies of €440 per hectare per year, the arithmetic — Mulkerrin says, then stops, because she has said this many times and it has not yet been arithmetic that decided anything.

IV · What Comes Next

An Atlas of Small Returns

There is no plausible future in which all of Connemara's degraded bog returns to mire. The roads have hardened the edges; the spruce plantations have acidified the runoff; the climate, regardless of every drain blocked tomorrow, is now warmer and flashier than the system evolved within. But there is a future, perhaps, of patches — of the seventy-one parcels that the 2023 National Peatlands Strategy identified as recoverable within twenty years, totalling some 18,400 hectares. That is roughly the size of the city of Dublin, and roughly four per cent of the original Connemara bog area.

Bríd Ó Conghaile, on the walk back, points to a patch of Cloonacartan where the sphagnum is, very visibly, electric green. "It came back," she says, "in three winters. Three winters." She does not say it as a victory. She says it as a fact one might use to argue with one's grandchildren, if one had the right grandchildren and the right argument. The bog, beneath us, exhales again. The rain, as promised, returns.
