Field Dispatches · Autumn 2024 Six Letters From The Edge

Field Notes

Across six continents and one drying inland sea, our correspondents this season filed shorter than usual — and sharper than usual. What follows are unembellished dispatches from places where ecological time is accelerating: peat lifted by helicopter, mangroves measured in centimetres lost, steppes set on fire to be saved. Read them together and a pattern emerges. Read them apart and the particulars hold.

Dispatch 01 · Congo Basin 0.7893° S, 23.6560° E

The Cuvette Centrale Holds Its Breath

— Filed by Adaeze Okwu, Mbandaka station

For the third consecutive dry season, peat moisture in the central basin has fallen below the 80% threshold considered safe for carbon stability. Concession maps redrawn in March now route logging roads within four kilometres of an unbroken Raphia palm corridor that hydrologists have spent two decades mapping by canoe.

The forest is still there. The water underneath it is leaving.

Local fishery cooperatives report catches of Hydrocynus down 41% year-on-year. Ministry hydrology bulletins have not been issued since June; staff cite fuel shortages and a stalled satellite link to Kinshasa.

Note Peat depths near Lokolama exceed 5.8 m — among the deepest measured in the tropics. — Dargie et al., Nature 2017
Note New concessions cover an area roughly the size of Belgium. — Global Forest Watch, 2024
Dispatch 02 · Sundarbans 21.9497° N, 89.1833° E

Salt Lines Reach The Sweet Wells

— Filed by Rohan Banerjee, Gosaba

The post-monsoon salinity gradient has moved 11 km inland since 2019. In the village of Satjelia, three of seven tube wells now run brackish through the dry months; rice yields on the polder behind them have collapsed to a quarter of pre-Aila levels. Women here measure the year by which crops they had to stop attempting.

We did not lose the land. The sea simply borrowed the soil.

Forest Department patrols counted 96 tiger movements through the buffer zone in the first half of 2024 — a thirty-year high, attributed to displacement from southern islands now uninhabitable to prey.

Note Mean tidal range at Hiron Point: 4.7 m, rising annually. — Bangladesh IWM, 2023
Note Embankment failures recorded at 38 sites since Cyclone Remal. — WBSDMA field log
Dispatch 03 · Patagonian Steppe 46.5947° S, 71.7259° W

Guanaco Counts Return To The Plateau

— Filed by Inés Caamaño, Río Mayo

Two decades after the wool-collapse abandoned an estimated 2.3 million hectares of estancia, the guanaco census on the central Santa Cruz plateau has crossed 110,000 head for the first time since records began. Restoration ecologists credit the unintentional rewilding to fence removal funded, paradoxically, by a now-defunct sheep cooperative.

The grass remembers what the ledgers forgot.

Puma sightings inside the Patagonia National Park corridor are up sixfold, though shepherds in adjacent valleys report sustained livestock losses and have petitioned for a managed cull this austral spring.

Note Stipa speciosa cover has rebounded 28% on monitored plots. — CONICET steppe survey, 2024
Note Wool prices peaked in 1991 and have not recovered in real terms. — INTA Bariloche
Dispatch 04 · Scottish Cairngorms 57.0700° N, 3.6700° W

The Pine Marten Walks South Again

— Filed by Eilidh MacRae, Glenmore

Camera traps installed along the Caledonian pine remnants of Abernethy have logged pine marten activity 22 km further south than recorded in 2018. The recovery is uneven: capercaillie leks within the same forest remain at six birds, down from forty-one a decade ago, and managers privately concede the species may not persist in Britain past 2035.

One predator returns to a forest that is forgetting its rarest bird.

Muirburn licensing reforms passed in May have already reduced the burned area on grouse moors abutting the park by 38%, but enforcement on private estates remains, as a ranger put it, "a matter of weather and faith."

Note Pinus sylvestris regeneration up 14% on de-stocked plots. — NatureScot, 2024
Note Capercaillie population: ~542 individuals UK-wide. — RSPB national survey
Dispatch 05 · Borneo Lowlands 1.5533° N, 110.3592° E

The Dipterocarp That Did Not Flower

— Filed by Cahaya Tan, Lambir Hills

For the second consecutive expected masting year, the canopy emergents of Lambir Hills have failed to synchronise. Researchers attribute the gap to anomalously high minimum night-time temperatures, which appear to suppress the thermal cue triggering simultaneous flowering across the Shorea genus. Seed predators — bearded pigs especially — have shifted ranges in response.

A forest that flowers together either lives or dies together.

An oil-palm replanting concession 9 km north has begun trialling shaded inter-row plantings of native Macaranga; early data suggests modest gains in beetle diversity but no measurable change in soil hydrology.

Note Last full mast event: 2019; previous gap 1998–2002. — Sakai & Itioka, Sarawak Forestry
Note Night-time temperature anomaly +1.8°C, July–Sept 2024. — MMD station Bintulu
Dispatch 06 · Aral Sea Recovery Zone 46.0000° N, 60.0000° E

Saxaul Holds The Salt In Place

— Filed by Madina Yusupova, Muynak

The Kokaral dam continues to hold. The North Aral has stabilised at roughly 27,000 km² of restored surface, and fishery catches from Aralsk landed 8,400 tonnes last season — the highest figure recorded since the dam's completion in 2005. Forty kilometres south, on the exposed Aralkum seabed, a Korean-funded saxaul plantation now covers 1.7 million hectares.

A sea will not come back. A coastline, sometimes, will.

Dust events still loft pesticide-laden sediments into the lower Amu Darya basin; pediatric respiratory clinics in Nukus report seasonal admissions running 60% above the 1990s baseline.

Note North Aral salinity dropped from 30 g/L to 17 g/L since 2008. — Micklin, J. Hydrology 2022
Note Saxaul survival on planted plots: ~62% after three years. — UNDP Aral programme

Field Correspondents

Six Bylines · Autumn 2024

Adaeze Okwu Congo Basin A Lagos-trained ecologist and three-time Dispatch contributor, Okwu has spent eight seasons mapping the hydrology of the Cuvette Centrale by pirogue. She writes from Mbandaka and Kinshasa, and teaches occasionally at the University of Kisangani. 12 dispatches filed · joined 2019
Rohan Banerjee Sundarbans Banerjee covers coastal Bengal for the Dispatch from a houseboat office moored at Gosaba. A former hydrologist with Jadavpur University, he reports principally on salinity intrusion, tiger movement, and the politics of cyclone reconstruction. 17 dispatches filed · joined 2017
Inés Caamaño Patagonia Based in Coyhaique, Caamaño is a wildlife biologist and writer whose work tracks the post-pastoral recovery of the southern steppe. She has contributed to El Mostrador, Anfibia, and the Patagonia Park monitoring programme since 2014. 9 dispatches filed · joined 2021
Eilidh MacRae Cairngorms MacRae is a Scottish naturalist and former NatureScot ranger who reports from Strathspey on highland forest restoration, muirburn policy, and the lingering question of what a wild Cairngorm should actually look like in the twenty-first century. 14 dispatches filed · joined 2018
Cahaya Tan Borneo Tan splits her time between Kuching and the Lambir Hills research station, where she has worked as a botanical field assistant since 2016. Her Dispatch reporting covers dipterocarp phenology, oil-palm transition agriculture, and lowland riverine ecology. 6 dispatches filed · joined 2022
Madina Yusupova Aral Sea A Karakalpak-born journalist based in Nukus, Yusupova reports on the long, partial repair of the Aral basin. Her work has appeared in Eurasianet, Le Monde diplomatique, and the Tashkent journal of arid-land studies. 11 dispatches filed · joined 2020
