The Divisumma That Learned to Breathe

A study of the Olivetti Divisumma 18 — its rubber membrane, its tropical-arc housing, and the moment industrial design conceded that machines could be soft.

ObjectDivisumma 18 DesignerMario Bellini ManufacturerIng. C. Olivetti & C., S.p.A. OriginIvrea, Italy Period1969 — 1976

There is, in the lower drawer of every serious design archive, a small yellow brick that hums when you touch it. The Divisumma 18, released by Olivetti in 1969 and designed by Mario Bellini at the age of thirty-three, is not a calculator so much as a thesis on what a machine should feel like to a hand that has never operated one.

Bellini's first move was a refusal. He refused the rigid plastic keycap. He refused the chrome bezel. He refused the carriage return as a lever that announced itself with metallic violence. In their place: a continuous sheet of yellow polyurethane stretched like skin over the keyboard, dimpled into thirty-eight individual depressions, each one rising slightly above the next in a soft topographic field.

01The membrane was vulcanized in a single pour. Bellini reportedly insisted on a single piece because, in his words, "a hand does not negotiate with seams."

The housing — a single arc of injection-molded ABS in a chromatic key Olivetti would later call giallo Bellini — broke decisively with the rectilinear logic of the Programma 101 (Sottsass, 1965) that preceded it on the company's catalogue. Where Sottsass had drawn a confident horizontal slab, Bellini drew a wedge that leaned toward the operator, as if the object itself were performing a small bow.

This was not, as later commentators have suggested, a concession to ergonomics in the contemporary clinical sense. Ergonomics in 1969 was still, in Italy, largely a manufacturing concern — how to arrange a line, not a wrist. Bellini's wedge was rhetorical. It announced that the machine had a front and a back, a public face and a service rear.

02Compare the Programma 101's bilateral symmetry. The Divisumma 18 is the first Olivetti object that faces you.

The Divisumma 18 is the moment Italian industrial design stopped imitating the machine and started imitating the body that would touch it.— Vanni Pasca, in Domus, March 1972

The printing mechanism — a small thermal head that scrolled a forty-character paper tape across the rear platen — was hidden inside the housing in a way that no previous Olivetti calculating machine had attempted. Earlier models displayed their mechanisms as proof of their seriousness. The Divisumma 18 concealed them as proof of its sophistication. To watch the tape emerge from the rear slot was to watch a thought leave the object.

The display itself was a single line of green vacuum-fluorescent digits, recessed eleven millimeters into the housing and angled at fifteen degrees from horizontal. The recession was not decorative. It controlled glare under the overhead fluorescent lighting that was, by 1970, standard in every Milanese accounting office.

03The 15° display angle was a contested decision. Olivetti's engineers wanted 22°; Bellini won by demonstrating that 15° matched the natural neck-flex of a seated bookkeeper.

What the Divisumma 18 inaugurated, and what its descendants — the Logos 50, the Lexikon 82, eventually the Praxis 35 — would refine and ultimately exhaust, was a particular conviction: that the surface of an industrial object is not its boundary but its argument. Touch it, and you have been argued with.

By 1976 the line was discontinued. The membrane, prone to yellowing in direct sun, had begun to fade in display windows across Europe. The wedge — by then widely imitated, badly, by competitors from Casio to Sharp — had ceased to read as radical. The object retreated into the museum collection from which it has not since emerged.

"A machine that is shaped like the hand that will touch it has already conceded that it works for the hand, not against it." — Bellini, in conversation with Hans-Ulrich Obrist, 2003.
