Cover Story · Nocturnal Systems

Rut Blees Luxemburg and the Argument That the City at Night Is Already a Grid.

Twenty-seven years on, the 1997 series London — A Modern Project remains the most disciplined attempt to read pavement, sodium light, and architectural seepage as a single legible typographic surface — a quiet rebuke to the daylight assumptions of orthodox grid theory.

By Idris Holm-Kovač Photography Editor Saoirse Lin Issue XXXVII Pages 14 — 41

Luxemburg photographs pavement the way Müller-Brockmann ruled a page: with an insistence that the substrate already contains its own measure, and that the designer's task is merely to find the angle from which the measure becomes visible. Her 1997 sequence — fifteen large-format colour exposures made between two and four in the morning along the Thames corridor, Hackney, and the Old Kent Road — proceeds from a single, almost stubborn premise. The orthogonal city, drained of pedestrians and rinsed in tungsten and mercury vapour, is a grid that has been waiting since the Greater London Council to be read.

The photographs do not document. They typeset. Sodium pools on wet asphalt become column rules; the diagonal of a kerb against a parked Mondeo becomes a baseline; manhole covers — six per frame, almost without exception — function as folio markers, anchoring the eye exactly where the gutter would fall in a Swiss editorial spread.

What is rarely said about Luxemburg, and what this magazine has come to suspect is the actual content of the work, is that the series was made in conscious dialogue with the print tradition she inherited at Goldsmiths: long exposures calibrated against the column widths of Domus, of Du, of the late Wim Crouwel catalogues she taught from. The night, in her hands, is a galley proof.

Consider the canonical image, Towering Inferno (1995, but routinely catalogued with the 1997 set). The Trellick Tower's balcony rhythm, photographed from below at a four-minute exposure, resolves into a 12-unit vertical structure. Count them. There are twelve. The coincidence is not a coincidence; Luxemburg has admitted, in a 2004 conversation with the photographer Thomas Demand, that she chose her vantage point by stepping backwards until the building "scanned correctly" — her phrase — against an imagined 24-pica gutter.

The argument of the cover story, then, is this: Luxemburg is not a photographer who happens to like grids. She is a grid theorist whose preferred instrument is a Sinar 4×5 and whose preferred medium is the inverse-square falloff of municipal lighting.

The implications, for a magazine such as ours, are not small. If the nocturnal city is already a typeset object, then a great deal of what passes for "grid design" in editorial work is in fact a kind of retracing — an after-the-fact ruling of lines that the world has been laying down, in tarmac and sodium, since the 1960s. Luxemburg's contribution is to have made the prior grid visible without the embarrassment of pointing at it.

She is helped, of course, by the time of night. Between 2:00 and 4:00 a.m. the rhetorical noise of the city — pedestrians, traffic, the moralising glare of daylight — drops away, leaving only the infrastructural skeleton. It is the same operation Josef Müller-Brockmann performed on the page when he removed the headline and looked at the column rule.

"The city at night is not photographed by Luxemburg; it is set, the way a paragraph is set — leaded, justified, and quietly ruled against an underlying field that pedestrians, by daylight, are paid not to see." — Idris Holm-Kovač, in this issue

The fifteen plates are not equal. Three carry the conceptual load — Liebeslied, Sotto Voce, and the untitled image catalogued as Plate VII — and the rest function as supporting matter, as folios and running heads function in a book. This is structural, not hierarchical: Luxemburg, in the 1998 Tate hang, insisted on the full sequence as a single object, and refused offers to sell individual prints to MoMA for nearly a decade on the grounds that "a paragraph is not a sentence."

To read the series as a grid system is, finally, to read it as a working document — a specimen book for a typographic practice that has not yet acquired its proper name. Folio Obscura proposes, here, the working term nocturnal setting, and offers the following gallery as a first inventory of the rules that govern it.

The remaining question is what to do with the argument. If Luxemburg has shown that the nocturnal city is already typeset, the editorial designer is left in an awkward position: either to imitate the city, which produces pastiche, or to acknowledge that the grid is not a designer's invention at all but a method of paying attention. Folio Obscura, of course, prefers the second reading.

It is, we suspect, the reading Luxemburg herself would prefer. She has spent thirty years insisting that her work is not about the city but about looking at the city — an insistence that aligns her, finally, with the line that runs from Tschichold's late conversion to humanist setting, through Crouwel's quiet doubts about the Stedelijk catalogues, to the present moment, in which the most rigorous grids being made anywhere are being made, perhaps, by people who do not call themselves designers at all.

The cover of this issue, accordingly, reproduces Plate VII at full bleed, without overlay and without caption. The grid is in it already; you have only to wait, as she did, between two and four.
