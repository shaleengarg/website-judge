Featured Opinion · Polemic № 04

The Grid Was Never Neutral. It Was Always a Politics in Disguise.

By Elsa Marković-Hartmann Senior Editor, Grid Studies 3,420 words · Reading time 14 min

Every grid carries a worldview folded into its gutters. To draw twelve columns is to inherit a thousand prior arguments about hierarchy, eligibility, and the right speed at which a reader should be permitted to move. The Swiss did not invent neutrality; they invented a particularly persuasive costume for it, and three generations of editorial designers have worn it without checking the lining.

The vocabulary betrays us. "Baseline." "Field." "Module." We borrow our terms from agriculture and infantry, then pretend the structure that results is a matter of pure proportion. It is not. A grid decides who appears at the top of the page and who is consigned to the marginal column. It dictates whose photograph runs four units wide and whose runs two. The grid is editorial governance; we have simply taught ourselves to call it geometry.

A grid that admits no exceptions is not a system. It is an ideology with kerning. — Marković-Hartmann, on the 8-column dogma

Consider the curious afterlife of Müller-Brockmann's Grid Systems in Graphic Design, a book whose calm pedagogical tone has been mistaken, for fifty years, for a description of the natural order. Brockmann himself was clear: the field system was a method, not a moral. Yet the rationalist inheritance has hardened into something closer to liturgy. Junior designers are taught to "respect the grid" before they are taught to ask what the grid believes.

The 1990s loosened the screws. Carson tore the field. Brody cantilevered the headline into the gutter. The deconstructionists, in their brief and necessary mischief, demonstrated that a magazine could survive — even thrive — without a single repeated baseline. What they did not do, and could not do, was provide a replacement vocabulary. The discipline retreated, after a decade of vandalism, to the safety of measurable order.

We are now in a third phase, less reported because less photogenic. The intrinsic grid — sized by content, negotiated by container queries, drawn at runtime — has begun to dissolve the very idea of a prescribed module. A page no longer "sits inside" a grid. It generates one, locally, each time it is rendered. The implications for editorial design are not yet understood.

The most radical grid of the next decade will be the one that disappears when nobody is looking at it. — On intrinsic, generative layout

What persists is the old confusion between order and authority. A grid imposes order; this is its job. But authority — the right to decide what order means — is bestowed by editors, art directors, publishers, and increasingly by software defaults written in California. To speak of the grid as if it were a neutral substrate is to launder authorship through arithmetic. It is a sleight of hand we should, finally, name.

If there is a dispatch to file from the front, it is this: the grid is alive, embattled, more interesting than at any point since 1968, and not at all what its inheritors have insisted. The young designers I admire most do not "follow the grid." They interrogate it, occasionally obey it, frequently ignore it, and — on the rare exceptional page — design one that has never existed before.

The remaining question is whether our institutions, our schools, and our trade press will catch up before the practice has moved on entirely. The evidence, as elsewhere in this issue, is mixed.

Explainer · Module 04.3

The Müller-Brockmann Field System, Diagrammed

After "Grid Systems," 1981 · plate 47
  1. AField unit (2×2): the elementary picture module. Brockmann derived its proportions from the typographic block, not the page.
  2. BCompound headline span: four fields merged horizontally; the dominant hierarchy device of the Zurich masthead style.
  3. CGutter rhythm: equal vertical and horizontal interstice. Sacred to the rationalists; routinely violated after 1985.
  4. DOuter margin: the most argued-about dimension in 20th-century print. Brockmann favoured generosity.
  5. EBaseline grid: typographic substrate beneath the field system; the two are independently tunable and frequently confused.
