STRATA & SEDIMENT · III

This catalogue is not a record of the exhibition — it is a parallel exhibition. Printed on Munken Pure 120gsm with a six-pass risograph cover and a single letterpress folio at its center, Volume III treats its own materiality as primary text. The reader who finishes the book has read the work; the reader who finishes the work has only begun the book. We have arranged the two so that neither resolves the other.

On Geological Patience

Essay I · pages 014–029 Reading time approx. 22 minutes

The sediments we admire in the cliff face were not assembled by intention but by duration. A risograph print, pulled six passes deep, asks of the printer something analogous: a willingness to let the layers misregister, to permit the seventh second of drying time before the eighth pass is laid down. There is no shortcut to that minute. We can simulate the look of layered ink in software, but we cannot simulate the patience that produced the ink. The image, in the end, is a record of waiting — a small geology made flat.

J. van Doelen, Editor's introduction, p. 014

"A book is the slowest medium we have left. We are reluctant, here, to make it faster." From the Editor's Foreword · p. 008

The Ethics of Edition

Essay II · pages 061–075 By Ines Brækhus, Curator

An edition is a promise about scarcity — but it is also a promise about distribution. To print 375 copies is to admit, plainly, that we expect 375 readers and no more. The mathematics is honest in a way the screen rarely is. Every numbered impression, signed and stamped at the back of this volume, is a record of a relationship: a print, a press, a paper, a person. The ethics begin in the count. To inflate the count is to dilute the relationship. To suppress it is to hide the labour. We have, in this third volume, decided to print the register in full.

Ines Brækhus, "The Ethics of Edition," p. 063

Ink as Archive

Essay III · pages 104–119 By Priya Menon, Contributing Artist

Soy-based risograph ink does not, in any meaningful sense, dry. It cures — slowly, over weeks, sometimes years — settling into the fibre of the page in a way that a museum conservator can date by touch. This is uncomfortable for the gallery and necessary for the work. The print continues to make itself after the printer has gone home. To treat ink as archive is to accept that the document is not finished when it leaves the press; it is finished, perhaps, never. The smudge on page 116 of your copy will not appear on mine. We have agreed, in advance, that this is good.

Priya Menon, "Ink as Archive," p. 107

"We did not write a catalogue about the work. We made a second work, in paper, that happens to remember the first." Editorial Statement · Volume III

A note on the binding.

The signatures are sewn rather than glued; the cover board is 2mm grey-brown chipboard, hand-wrapped in a single sheet of risograph-printed Munken stock. Each spine is numbered by hand in pencil at the head, between the band and the foredge. Where the pencil mark is faint, it has not been retouched.
