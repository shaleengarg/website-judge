Section 03 — The Hand & The Press

Four makers, four pressures.

The artists currently represented by Obsidian Fold work at the threshold of pigment and paper. Each profile below records a practice in its own register — geographies, materials, and the marks left by patient repetition.

Maren Holt

Norway  ·  Risograph & salt-bleached pigment on Hahnemühle

Holt works at the slow end of process. Her risographs are pulled in editions of fifteen across four ink passes, each layer cured for forty-eight hours before the next is registered. The work that emerges is less an image than a deposition — strata of fluorescent rose, federal blue, and the brown that occurs only when two of her chosen inks fail to settle.

For Holt, registration error is not a flaw to be designed around but a record of weather. Her Bergen studio is unheated until October; the paper expands and contracts with the harbour fog, and the press records this as a soft halo at every edge.

“I have stopped apologising for the misalignment. The misalignment is the year the print was made.” — Maren Holt, in conversation, 2023

Her current series, Saltvann, was produced over fourteen months and exists in two states: an edition pulled in summer light and a winter counterpart printed on the same plates. Shown side by side, they describe a year in the life of a single sheet.

Soo-Jin Bae

South Korea  ·  Letterpress on kozo, polymer & metal type

Bae sets her own type. She has milled three full-pica fonts from cherrywood and is currently working a fourth in poplar. Her prints are typographic but rarely legible; words are pressed deep into damp kozo until they pass through to the verso, becoming relief on one side and impression on the other. The viewer is asked to choose a face.

Influences arrive through translation. Bae has spoken of hanji as a substrate that refuses haste — a sheet that punishes pressure misapplied — and her practice has come to depend on that punishment.

“A letter that does not bruise the paper has not committed to being read.” — Soo-Jin Bae, artist's notes, 2024

Recent works incorporate fragments of pre-modern Korean ledgers; the printed verso of one suggests an inventory of millet, the recto a love letter. Bae will not say which is the source.

Darius Vael

Belgium  ·  Risograph & screenprint on Munken Pure

Vael builds compositions algorithmically and prints them by hand. He writes short generative scripts to produce moiré fields and gradient interference, then commits to a single output — never the most beautiful, always the one nearest to failing — and pulls the edition through a temperamental MZ1090 he refuses to service.

The tension in his work sits between calculation and contingency. The drum will drop ink; the script will not. Vael accepts the drop.

“The machine has opinions about what I have asked of it. I have learned to listen.” — Darius Vael, studio interview, 2022

His Interference Studies are routinely shown unframed, taped to the wall with linen hinges. He prefers that the paper continue to move in the room's air.

Priya Menon

United Kingdom  ·  Letterpress, hand-cut woodblock, indigo

Menon prints in indigo she ferments herself. The vat lives in a cupboard in Govanhill and is replenished on a schedule she will not disclose; the colour that arrives on the paper depends on the day of the week and the weather. Her woodblocks, cut from reclaimed beech, are deliberately fragile — she has, on two occasions, printed a block to destruction.

The work is, in her own description, an inheritance argument. Menon's grandmother dyed cloth in Kerala; the prints are a way of asking what survives translation across an ocean, a generation, and a press.

“Indigo is the only blue that goes onto the paper green and decides, in the air, what it wishes to become.” — Priya Menon, gallery talk, 2024

She is currently at work on Six Returns, a suite of large-format prints in which the same block is pulled in six successive vats, each weaker than the last — a sequence describing the decay of a colour and, she suggests, of a memory.
