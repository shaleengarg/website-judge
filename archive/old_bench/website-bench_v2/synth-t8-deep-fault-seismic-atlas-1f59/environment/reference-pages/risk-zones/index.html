Chapter 03 — Risk Zones

The Codes That Were Never Written, And The Cities Living Inside The Margin Of Error

From Padang's seafront mosques to Seaside's beachfront motels, three Pacific Rim communities expose a quiet pattern: building codes that exist on paper but vanish at the foundation line.

Three Cities, One Margin Of Error

In Padang, a coastal city of 920,000 on Sumatra's western edge, the building code adopted in 2010 was hailed as a regional template. Fifteen years later, structural surveys conducted by the West Sumatra Disaster Mitigation Agency show that fewer than one in four post-2010 buildings comply with the seismic provisions that were supposed to make them survivable. The rest are concrete frames with masonry infill, the same brittle typology that collapsed in the 2009 earthquake and killed at least 1,117 people.

The problem is not the absence of rules. It is the absence of enforcement, capacity, and political memory. Inspectors in Padang are responsible, on average, for 340 active permits each. Most never visit a site after the foundation pour. Engineers we interviewed described a routine of signing off on drawings they had not reviewed for a fee equal to a single day's wages.

"We are not building for the next earthquake. We are building against the last one — and even that, badly." — Dr. Hadi Wirawan, Structural Engineer, Andalas University

Seaside, Oregon: A Town On A Beach On A Subduction Zone

Six thousand miles east, the Oregon coastal town of Seaside has a different problem: a code that is among the most sophisticated in the world, applied to a building stock that predates it by half a century. A 2022 inventory by the Oregon Department of Geology and Mineral Industries found that 71% of Seaside's downtown structures were built before 1974, when seismic provisions for Cascadia were essentially non-existent. The tsunami inundation zone — modeled at up to 27 feet of run-up — overlaps almost perfectly with the town's commercial core, its two K-12 schools, and its only hospital.

Retrofitting, in theory, is straightforward. In practice, the cost of upgrading a single unreinforced masonry building in Seaside averages $340 per square foot, against a commercial property tax base too thin to support municipal bonds at the scale required. Voters rejected a $99 million school relocation measure in 2016 and again, narrowly, in 2022. The new high school remains inside the inundation zone.

Batangas: The Forgotten Western Flank

The Manila Trench runs parallel to the western coast of Luzon, capable, by consensus modeling, of generating an M8.2 rupture with tsunami arrival times of under 15 minutes at Batangas City. The Philippine National Building Code references seismic design, but its 1977 base text — amended but never fundamentally rewritten — does not address near-field subduction motion. Local officials in Batangas confirmed to us that no provincial drill scenario has yet been run for a Manila Trench event; planning continues to focus on the West Valley Fault, 110 kilometers inland.

"The fault we are not drilling for is the fault we will not survive." — Civil Defense Officer, Region IV-A · Anonymized

What unites these three cities is not a single failure but a shared pattern: a code, a coast, and a clock. The codes were written. The coasts kept developing. And the clock — the recurrence interval that geologists measure in centuries — does not negotiate with municipal budgets.

In the gauges below, we score each cluster on four axes: early-warning infrastructure, structural compliance, public-drill cadence, and evacuation route capacity. None of the four exceed a score of 6 out of 10. Two fall below 4. The remaining text of this chapter, available in the print edition, examines the policy levers that could close those gaps within a single budget cycle — if the political will to pull them exists.
