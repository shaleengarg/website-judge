§ 01 — Overview

The quiet arc where the Pacific is still tightening.

By the Deep Fault Desk
Seismology · Policy · Field Reporting
Manila · Wellington · Astoria

"The fault that worries us most is the one that hasn't broken in living memory."
The Lede — 660 words

Three subduction zones, separated by thousands of kilometers of ocean, share a single uncomfortable fact: they are overdue, under-instrumented, and absent from the political imagination of the cities they will eventually destroy. Cascadia, off the coast of Oregon and Washington, has not produced a great-magnitude earthquake since January 26, 1700 — a date fixed by a tsunami that crossed the Pacific and was logged by Edo-era scribes in Japan. The interval implied by paleoseismic trench studies is between 243 and 500 years. We are inside that window.

Eight thousand kilometers south, the Hikurangi Margin presses against New Zealand's North Island. Its locked patch beneath the Wairarapa coast stores strain at a rate geodesists describe, plainly, as "alarming." A 2023 GNS Science report estimated a 26% probability of a magnitude 8.0-plus rupture within fifty years. Wellington — a capital city of 215,000 — sits within reach of the resulting tsunami in under seven minutes.

The third corridor, the Manila Trench, runs the length of Luzon's western flank and is the least monitored of the three. Despite proximity to a metropolitan region of 13 million, fewer than 40 broadband seismometers cover the offshore segment. Filipino seismologists have publicly stated, in language unusual for a government agency, that they are "flying blind." A magnitude 8.2 here would generate a basin-wide tsunami affecting Vietnam, southern China, and the entire South China Sea littoral.

What unites these three zones is not geology — the rocks differ, the slip rates differ, the convergence angles differ — but the political pattern around them. Each sits beneath a coastline where building codes lag the science by two to four decades. Each is governed by emergency-management agencies whose budgets have flatlined or contracted in real terms since 2015. Each has produced, in the last five years, at least one internal memo warning of inadequate preparedness; in each case, those memos were quietly filed.

This report does not predict an earthquake. Prediction, in the sense the public means it, remains scientifically impossible. What this report does is map the gap between what is known and what is being done — a gap that, on the present trajectory, will be closed not by policy but by an event. The four chapters that follow walk that gap in detail: the structural geology of the corridors themselves; the cities whose codes leave them most exposed; the survivors of the last rehearsal, in Padang in 2009; and the specific reforms that could, even now, reduce the death toll of the rupture that is coming.

We have spent eighteen months on this reporting. We interviewed 47 seismologists, 22 emergency planners, and 31 survivors. We obtained, through freedom-of-information requests in four jurisdictions, internal risk assessments that have not previously been published. Where the data is uncertain, we say so. Where it is not, we have not softened it.
