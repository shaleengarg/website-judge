Essays & Criticism

Long-form arguments, close readings, and dispatches from the edges of contemporary literary and cultural life.

The Slow Pulse of the Reissued Novel

by Helena Marchetti

When a forgotten 1973 novel returns to bookshop tables, what changes is not the prose but the silence around it. Marchetti traces how publishers have learned to manufacture rediscovery, and asks whether nostalgia has become our most durable critical lens.

Against the Tidy Sentence

by Daniel Okafor

A polemic in favor of difficulty. The contemporary essay, Okafor argues, has been smoothed into a kind of consumer object — paragraphs engineered for the scrolling thumb. He proposes a return to syntax that resists, delays, and occasionally wounds the reader.

Cities That Read Themselves

by Priya Shenoy

From Lisbon to Lahore, certain cities have always written their own literature in the margins of public space. Shenoy walks four such cities, listening for the moment when graffiti, bus-stop poetry, and overheard conversation cohere into something resembling a regional canon.

On Letters Never Sent

by August Vlahos

The unsent letter is its own minor genre — confession without audience, rehearsal without performance. Vlahos surveys the archives of three twentieth-century writers whose drawers contained more honest work than their published pages ever did, and considers what we owe such documents.

The Translator as Second Author

by Mira Aldebaran

Translation has always been collaboration with the dead, but Aldebaran insists it is also authorship of the living. She examines three contested retranslations of Russian and Portuguese classics, and asks whether our publishing conventions have finally caught up to the labor involved.

Notes on the Domestic Uncanny

by Theodora Lin

A generation of younger novelists has turned the kitchen, the hallway, and the unlit stairwell into sites of genuine dread. Lin reads four recent debut novels alongside Bachelard, finding a renewed seriousness about domestic space as a stage for psychological weather.

What the Footnote Knows

by Reuben Castellanos

Footnotes are where prose admits its doubts. Castellanos, a working historian, defends the lowly note against editors who would shoo it into endnotes or abolish it entirely, and argues that argument itself depends upon the visible apparatus of reading.

The Quiet Politics of the Library Card

by Naomi Bridgewater

Public libraries persist as one of the last unbilled rooms in civic life. Bridgewater visits four branches in three countries, tracing how access to the printed page has become — improbably, stubbornly — a frontline argument about citizenship, time, and attention.
