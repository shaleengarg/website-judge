About

Petra Voss

I trained as a geologist before I learned to trust the long sentence over the field sketch. For nine years I worked on alpine surveys in the Eastern Alps and the Caucasus, reading rock the way some people read weather — by texture, by what it had survived. Somewhere between a season in the Karakoram and a winter spent rewriting a report nobody would read, I began keeping a different kind of notebook. The essays grew out of those notebooks. The blog grew out of the essays.

I've summited Mont Blanc, Aconcagua, Denali, and a handful of more obscure peaks in Kyrgyzstan and Norway that meant more to me than the famous ones. I am not interested in chasing eight-thousanders. The mountains I return to are the ones that ask the same question twice. I climb slowly, mostly with one or two partners, and I walk the approaches whenever the geography allows it — trains, ferries, the long way around. Slow travel has become, almost by accident, the spine of how I think.

What I hope you take from these pages is small and specific: that attention is a form of altitude, that a careful sentence and a careful step share a discipline, and that the world is still mostly composed of places where nothing in particular is happening — which is where the best thinking tends to live. If anything here moves you to put on boots, or to write your own version of it down, I will consider the blog to have done its work.
