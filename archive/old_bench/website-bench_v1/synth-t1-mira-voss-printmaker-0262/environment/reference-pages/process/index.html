Process

Method Notes — Schwarzschnitt Studio

Drawing

Every print begins on cheap newsprint with a 2B pencil. I work from site sketches and memory rather than photographs, letting the proportions of cooling towers, viaducts, and gantries settle through repeated drafts. A finished drawing is usually the seventh or eighth attempt, kept rough at the edges so that the eventual cut still has somewhere to go.

The drawing is transferred to the block by rubbing the reverse of the sheet with a bone folder — pencil-to-paper, no solvents, no carbon. The reversal is part of the thinking: structures lean the other way, the light shifts, and I redraw directly onto the block in soft graphite before any tool touches the surface.

Cutting

I cut on Bavarian pearwood for woodcuts and grey battleship linoleum for finer line work. The tools are a small set of Japanese hangi-to and a single European U-gouge inherited from my first teacher in Leipzig. I sharpen daily on a 6000-grit waterstone; the studio rule is that a dull tool gets put away rather than forced.

Cutting moves from the largest negative areas inward, leaving the architectural skeleton for last. I take regular proofs on cheap kozo to test weight and balance, and I am willing to abandon a block at any stage — three of the editions on the prints page are second versions of compositions that failed at this point.

Printing

Inks are Japanese water-based pigments, mixed in small porcelain dishes and applied with a flat hake brush rather than a roller. The water content is adjusted by the hour according to humidity in the studio; in a Berlin winter this means rewetting the block four or five times across a single edition.

Paper is handmade Awagami kozo, dampened overnight between blotters. Each sheet is hand-burnished with a baren, then pulled through a vintage 1887 Albion press for the final, even impression. A run of thirty prints takes roughly four working days, and no two sheets in an edition are mechanically identical.

Editions are signed, numbered, and dated in pencil on the lower margin, and each print is documented in a bound studio ledger that travels with the work.
