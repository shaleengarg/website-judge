XIVFrom II. Foundry Light

Molten Cadastre

Céleste Vaudreuil · Sorel-Tracy
Marginalia In November 1944, some 4,300 workers — many of them women from the surrounding parishes — clocked into the Sorel Industries shell plant on the south bank of the St. Lawrence. The cadastre, the old surveyor's land register, was redrawn in iron that winter; entire rangs vanished into the foundry's payroll. My grandmother kept her timecard folded inside a missal until 1981.

Before the river learned the name of iron
it learned the names of women
who walked the rang at four
their lunch pails ticking like small barometers
against the cold

The foundry kept its own cadastre
a ledger of parcels measured
not in arpents but in shifts
each acre a lung each lung a quota
stamped beside a number

and what the surveyor could not chain
the smoke inherited

My grandmother's hands
were a map of the south shore
calloused at Saint-Joseph-de-Sorel
scarred where the lathe at Tracy
had taken its tithe of skin

She said the shells were daughters
she said she counted them
the way one counts rosaries
one for each brother sent
one for each field gone fallow

What is a cadastre but an argument
between memory and the assessor
between the river that will not stay
and the survey that insists it must
here is the lot here is the line

I have inherited only the smoke
a longitude without latitude
a measure that drifts
like the women drifted home at dawn
their kerchiefs already grey with morning
