CHAPTER 02 // EXPOSURE

The Latent Image

On silver halide emulsions, suspended chemistry, and the moment before a photograph exists.
§ 2.1 — Suspension

Chemistry of Almost

"The image exists before it is seen — suspended, waiting for light to become memory."

A silver halide crystal is, in its undisturbed state, a lattice of indifference. It does not yet know what it will record. When a photon strikes it, an electron is freed, drifts to a sensitivity speck, and reduces a single silver ion to a metallic atom. Four such atoms are enough. From four atoms a future image begins to insist on itself.

This is the latent image: invisible, undeveloped, statistically real. The photograph is already there in the film, embedded in arrangements of atoms too few to see, waiting on a chemical bath to amplify what light has already decided. We do not develop the image so much as confirm it. The shutter is the only act of authorship; everything afterward is translation.

What interests me about exposure is its quiet duration. Even when the shutter is closed, the latent image continues to drift — slowly fading, recombining, decaying with a half-life of its own. A roll left in a drawer for ten years remembers less than it did the morning after. Memory leaks from emulsion the way it leaks from us: not at once, but at a rate.

Heliography

A silver-iodide mirror, developed in mercury vapor. One-of-a-kind, mercurial, lethal to its makers.

Gelatin Dry Plate

Maddox suspends silver bromide in gelatin. Photography becomes portable; exposures shorten to seconds.

Kodachrome

A three-layer color emulsion, developed by dye couplers. Color memory becomes commercial, stable, mass-produced.

Sasson's 0.01-megapixel CCD prototype at Kodak. The latent image migrates from silver to silicon, ending one chemistry, beginning another.

Three Facts About Silver

FIG. 2.B  //  CHEMICAL FACT CARDS
FACT 01 // SENSITIVITY

Four Atoms Are Enough

A silver halide crystal becomes developable once roughly four reduced silver atoms cluster at a single sensitivity speck. Below that threshold, the latent image fades; above it, the crystal will amplify by a factor of one billion in the developer bath.

AgBr + hν → Ag⁰ + Br⁰
FACT 02 // FIXATION

The Bath That Forgets

Sodium thiosulfate — "hypo" — dissolves unexposed halides into soluble silver complexes. Fixation is photography's act of editing memory: it removes everything the light did not touch, leaving only what was seen.

AgBr + 2 Na₂S₂O₃ → Na₃[Ag(S₂O₃)₂] + NaBr
FACT 03 // DECAY

The Half-Life of a Negative

Even archivally processed silver gelatin degrades. Residual thiosulfate, atmospheric sulfur, and humidity attack the image at a measurable rate. A well-kept negative has a working half-life of roughly one hundred years.

t½ ≈ 100 yr   @ 20°C, 30% RH
