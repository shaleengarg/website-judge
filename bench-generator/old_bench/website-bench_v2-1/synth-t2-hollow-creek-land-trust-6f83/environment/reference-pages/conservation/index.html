Conservation in Practice

From ridgeline headwaters to creek-bottom hardwoods, our work protects the connected fabric of Southern Appalachian habitat — one easement, one prescribed burn, one water sample at a time.

How We Protect Land

Hollow Creek Land Trust safeguards forested ridgeline and creek-bottom habitat across five Appalachian counties. We work directly with families, farmers, and forest owners who want to keep their land intact for the generations to come — not as museum pieces, but as living, working landscapes.

Conservation Easements

A conservation easement is a voluntary, permanent legal agreement between a landowner and the Trust that limits certain types of development while keeping the land in private ownership. Owners may continue to farm, sustainably harvest timber, hunt, hike, and pass the land to their heirs. We currently hold 47 easements covering more than 12,400 acres, and we monitor every protected parcel on the ground each year.

Forest Management Plans

Working with state foresters and consulting biologists, we help landowners draft management plans tailored to oak-hickory and cove hardwood systems. Plans address invasive species control, native regeneration, snag retention for wildlife, and selective harvest practices that maintain canopy structure and soil health across multi-decade horizons.

Water Quality Monitoring

Volunteers and staff sample 22 stream reaches across the Hollow Creek watershed four times a year, tracking macroinvertebrate diversity, turbidity, temperature, and dissolved oxygen. The data feeds a public dataset shared with the regional watershed authority and informs where we prioritize riparian buffer restoration on partner properties.
