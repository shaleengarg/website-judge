Apparatus  ·  Glossary & Notes

Notes Toward
the Smoke

Fourteen contextual entries — geographical, industrial, and Québécois vernacular — keyed to terms appearing throughout The Longitude of Smoke.

Read in any order. The collection assumes the river knows its own vocabulary; these notes are for the reader who has not yet stood on the wharf at Sorel-Tracy in late November.
01
Sorel-Tracy

Industrial town at the confluence of the Richelieu and St. Lawrence rivers; the poet's birthplace and the geographic anchor of section II, Foundry Light. Home to the Marine Industries shipyards until their dismantling in 1986.

02
Batture

Québécois French for the tidal flats exposed at low water along the St. Lawrence estuary — a zone neither river nor shore. Recurs as a structuring image in poems VII and XIX.

03
QIT-Fer et Titane

Titanium dioxide smelter operating in Sorel-Tracy since 1950. The pale plume visible from the wharf is the literal referent of the collection's title and the subject of the opening poem.

04
Cadastre

A public register of land parcels and their boundaries. In Molten Cadastre the word is deformed to describe the foundry floor as a map of property re-cast in iron.

"We inherited the river the way one inherits a bruise — without ceremony, without choosing the colour it would become." — from XI · Inventory of the Slow Thaw
05
Goélette

A small wooden cargo schooner once used to haul lumber and pulp along the lower St. Lawrence. Largely vanished by 1970; their hulks remain visible at low tide near Île aux Coudres.

06
Lac Saint-Pierre

A widening of the St. Lawrence between Sorel and Trois-Rivières; UNESCO biosphere reserve. Its winter ice is the setting of the long central poem Argentine of the Frozen Channels.

07
Frasil

Fine spicules of ice that form in turbulent, supercooled water before consolidating into sheet ice. The poet uses the term as a verb in poem XVI: "the years frasil along the bank."

08
Mouleur

Foundry moulder; the worker who prepares the sand cast into which molten metal is poured. The poet's maternal grandfather worked as a mouleur at Sorel Steel from 1938 to 1971.

09
Acier-Bessemer

Bessemer-process steel; obsolete by the time the collection's industrial scenes are set, but the term survives in regional speech to denote any cheaply-made or brittle thing.

10
Marée d'équinoxe

The equinoctial tide, highest of the year on the lower estuary. Forms the temporal frame of the closing section, Estuary, in which each poem is dated to a specific tide.

"What the smelter exhales the river inhales — there is no third party to this respiration, only us standing at the rail counting our breath in someone else's measure." — from III · Plume Catechism
11
Beluga

The white whale of the St. Lawrence; a non-migratory population of roughly 900, listed as endangered. Their bodies, when stranded, are classified as toxic waste under Canadian law — a fact the collection refuses to let pass without elegy.

12
Bardeau

Wooden shingle; by metonymy, the modest gabled houses of the Sorel suburbs. The poet's childhood home, demolished in 2009, is described in poem XX as "a bardeau remembering its tree."

13
Drague

Dredge; the vessels that maintain the shipping channel to Montréal. Their slow night-passage past Sorel is invoked as a counter-rhythm to the foundry whistle in the volume's penultimate poem.

14
Longitude

A meridian; here, the imagined line running north from the smelter stack through the poet's body and into the cold mouth of the Gulf. The book's organising fiction: that grief, like geography, can be measured westward from a fixed plume.
