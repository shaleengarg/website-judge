The Poet — b. 1981, Sorel-Tracy

Céleste
Vaudreuil.

Portrait — Rivière Richelieu, 2021

Before the poems, there was the ledger room: a long iron shelf in the records annex of Aciers Sorel, where Céleste Vaudreuil spent four winters cataloguing bills of lading from the foundry's final operating decades. The mill was already closing in pieces. She was nineteen, the archivist was seventy, and the building smelled of slag, paper, and the river — a smell, she has said, that taught her everything she would later understand about line breaks.

Vaudreuil was born in Sorel-Tracy in 1981, the granddaughter of a millwright and a midwife who corresponded for forty years across the St. Lawrence delta. She studied geography at Université Laval before turning to poetry, and her early work appeared in Estuaire, Liberté, and The Malahat Review. She translates from Québécois French and English with what one editor called "a tide-gauge ear."

"Every poem is a tide gauge recording an absence — the absence of a foundry, a grandmother, a river that has not yet decided whether to be salt." — Paris Review, Issue 241

Her debut collection, The Longitude of Smoke, gathers twenty-three poems written between 2014 and 2022, structured as a downriver passage from inland silt through industrial light to estuary. The book's middle section draws directly on the archive years, while its closing movement is distilled from letters Vaudreuil wrote to her late grandmother, Yvette Lemay, and never sent.

She lives in Trois-Rivières, teaches a winter seminar on documentary poetics at Université du Québec, and keeps a small herbarium of grasses native to the delta's tidal flats. The Longitude of Smoke is her first book.
