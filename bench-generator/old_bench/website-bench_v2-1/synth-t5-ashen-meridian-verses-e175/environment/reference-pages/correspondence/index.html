Prose Archive — Three Letters

Letters Never Sent Downriver

Drafts addressed to Madeleine Vaudreuil (1919–2007), composed on the kitchen table at Sorel-Tracy between 2018 and 2021. None were posted. All were saved.
I. First Letter
Sorel-Tracy — 14 November 2018
Grand-maman,

The foundry shut its east gate this morning and a heron stood inside the chain-link as though it had been invited. I thought of you and the way you used to say the river kept appointments the men did not.

I have begun copying out the inventory ledgers from the archive — pig iron, slag, the names of the women who weighed it. Your name is on a page from 1944. You never told me you worked the scales. You told me only about the bread.

I am trying to write a poem with the cadence of your French, but the English keeps lowering its voice. Every line I finish smells of cold metal. Perhaps that is the inheritance.

The ice has not yet come. When it does I will walk out to where the channel narrows and read this aloud and not send it.

Yours in the long afternoon, Céleste
II. Second Letter
Montréal — 3 March 2020
Chère grand-maman,

I dreamt last night that the munitions plant had been rebuilt from memory rather than from brick, and you were inside it folding cartridges into envelopes, smiling like a girl. I woke up before I could ask you the address.

The city is hushed in a way I have only read about in your letters from the polio summer. I have been reading those letters again — the ones Maman kept in the cedar box — and I notice you wrote downriver the way other people write downstairs. Always a direction, always a falling.

I have finished eleven poems. Section II is called Foundry Light. I have stolen the phrase from a remark you made about a saint whose name I cannot recover. Forgive me. Or do not. The thief is already past the gate.

With my smaller hands in yours, Céleste
III. Third Letter
Île aux Coudres — 22 September 2021
Madeleine,

I am calling you by your name now because the book is almost finished and I want to address you as a peer of the dead, which is to say plainly. The estuary is wider here than language permits. The tide turns twice and forgets nothing.

I have written a third section called Estuary. It contains the poems I could not write while you were alive and the poems I could not have written had you not been. Both kinds. They sit in the same drawer.

The publisher in Toronto has asked for a dedication. I have not yet decided whether to name you on the page or leave the page blank and let the smoke do it. You would prefer the blank, I think. You preferred most things blank.

The river is the color of a coin left in rain. I am going to put this letter in the cedar box with the others and not send it, as we have agreed without ever agreeing.

Ta petite-fille, toujours, Céleste V.

These three letters form the prose skeleton from which the collection's third section, Estuary, was distilled. The originals remain in a cedar box in Sorel-Tracy; what appears above is a reading copy prepared by the poet for the Ashen Meridian archive. No reply was ever expected, and none, of course, arrived.
