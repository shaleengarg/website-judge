§ 1.0   THE THRESHOLD OF THE LATENT

Before the image, the willingness of the image to appear.

A photograph begins not with the shutter but with a threshold — the precise quantity of light required to disturb a single grain of silver halide into willingness. Below that threshold there is nothing. Above it, the world begins to record itself, particle by particle, into a chemistry that does not yet know what it has become.

This essay traces five such thresholds. It is concerned with what analog photography can teach us about memory: not the digital fiction of perfect recall, but the slower, lossier work of substances that change as they are touched by time. Film fades. Prints yellow. The image is never finished arriving; it is also, from the moment of its making, always beginning to leave.

What follows is a sequence of chapters that move from the chemistry of exposure, through the rituals of the darkroom, into the grain itself, and finally toward the afterimage — the photograph we keep without keeping, the one that develops inside us long after the negative is lost.

Read in order, or do not. Each chapter is a separate exposure on the same length of film.
