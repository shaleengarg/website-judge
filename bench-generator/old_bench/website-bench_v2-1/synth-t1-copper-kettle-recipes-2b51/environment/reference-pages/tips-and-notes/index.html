Cook's Notes

Three small lessons that turn a good goulash into a great one.

Paprika Quality Matters

This dish lives or dies by the paprika. Use sweet Hungarian paprika — Pride of Szeged or Szegedi Édesnemes if you can find it — and make sure the tin is no older than a year. Stale paprika tastes like sawdust and will leave your goulash flat and dull-colored.

Don't Rush the Simmer

Once you stir the paprika into the onions, never let the pot reach a hard boil — high heat scorches paprika and turns it bitter in seconds. Keep the goulash at the gentlest possible simmer, with just a lazy bubble or two breaking the surface, for the full two and a half hours. The beef should give way to a spoon, not a knife.

Make It Ahead

Goulash is one of those rare stews that tastes noticeably better on day two. Cool it completely, refrigerate overnight, and reheat slowly the next afternoon — the paprika deepens and the beef drinks up more of the broth. If anything, make a double batch and freeze half in flat containers for up to three months.
