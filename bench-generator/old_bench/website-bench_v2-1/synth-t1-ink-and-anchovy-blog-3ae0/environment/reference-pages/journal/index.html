14 November 2024

A jar of salt-cured anchovies, and what to do at midnight

The fish arrived in a wooden crate from a friend in Cetara, packed under so much coarse salt that the lid had to be pried up with the back of a butter knife. I rinsed three of them under cold water at half past eleven, laid them on a square of brown paper, and ate them standing up over the sink with a slice of yesterday's bread.

There is a particular pleasure in eating something that has been waiting patiently in salt for a year. The flesh is dense, slightly meaty, the flavor almost embarrassingly direct. I keep meaning to write a longer piece about preservation as a moral posture — about the patience required to bury something now and trust your future self to deserve it — but tonight I just wanted dinner.

7 November 2024

Reading Pliny in the kitchen, badly

My Latin is rusty in the way a good cast-iron pan is rusty: still functional, mostly, but requiring a great deal of scrubbing before anything useful comes out. I have been working through Book XIX of the Naturalis Historia in twenty-minute increments, propped against the kettle, while waiting for things to boil.

Pliny on onions is everything you'd want him to be: dogmatic, slightly wrong, and oddly tender. He believes onions cure insomnia, dim vision, and dog bites. I tried one of the recipes last night — onions stewed slowly in vinegar and honey — and can confirm it cures nothing, but it does pair beautifully with a piece of grilled mackerel.

29 October 2024

The 6:15 ferry to Sandviken, in fog

I left the flat before the bakery opened, which I almost never do, because I wanted to be on the first crossing. The fjord was the color of pewter and the gulls were not yet awake. I drank a thermos of coffee on the upper deck and felt, briefly, like a character in a novel I wouldn't necessarily want to read.

Slow travel, I've decided, is mostly a question of being willing to be cold for slightly longer than is sensible. The reward is that you notice things: a man unloading crates of cod, a child counting the pilings as we passed, the precise moment the fog lifted off the warehouses and the world resumed its ordinary colors.

21 October 2024

On buying a third copy of the same cookbook

This is the third copy of Elizabeth David's Italian Food I have owned. The first was lent and never returned; the second fell into a bath, which seems on reflection like exactly the sort of thing that would happen to a book about Italian food in Norway in February.

The new copy is a 1989 Penguin paperback, found in a shop on Øvregaten for forty kroner. The previous owner has underlined every reference to anchovies, which is either a sign that we are kindred spirits or that I should be more worried about who I am becoming.

I read the chapter on Liguria over breakfast and started a shopping list I have no real intention of completing.

12 October 2024

Caponata, three ways, none of them mine

I have been testing caponata recipes for an essay I'm trying to write, and the kitchen smells, permanently now, of vinegar and warm aubergine. So far I've made a Palermitan version with pine nuts and cocoa, a more austere one from a 1950s pamphlet, and an outlier from a Sicilian friend's grandmother that involves octopus and absolutely no celery.

None of them are mine, which is the point. I am circling something — a way of writing about a dish that doesn't pretend I invented it, or improved it, or even fully understand it. Writing about food, I'm finding, is mostly the practice of admitting what you owe.
