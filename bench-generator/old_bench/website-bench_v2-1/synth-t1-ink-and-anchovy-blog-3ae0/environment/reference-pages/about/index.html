A short life with salt in it

My name is Marta Solberg. I spent the first decade of my working life as a fisheries journalist, mostly covering quota disputes, trawler economics, and the long, weather-bitten lives of the people who pull protein out of the North Atlantic for a living. It was a beat that taught me to take notes in the rain and to distrust press releases that smelled too clean.

In 2019 I moved from Oslo to Bergen — chasing, in roughly equal measure, cheaper rent, wetter weather, and a kitchen with a window that looked at something other than another kitchen. The move coincided with a quieter pivot: away from policy reporting and toward food writing, which is to say toward the same subjects from a softer angle. Cod is still cod. But you can also braise it.

These days I write about Mediterranean cooking, mostly Sicilian and Catalan, with the occasional detour through Provence when the mood is right. I am preoccupied — my partner would say obsessed — with anchovy-based condiments: colatura, garum, the salted fillets sold in tins so beautiful I keep the empties. I am also, less defensibly, working my way slowly through the Stoics, particularly Seneca, whom I find consoling on the subject of weather and bad dinners.

The name Ink & Anchovy is partly a joke about the contents of my desk and partly a serious description of two things I cannot stop thinking about: the written word and the small, oily fish that has, for some three thousand years, been quietly improving everything it touches. I think of this blog as a commonplace book — somewhere between a recipe drawer and a marginalia notebook — and I am grateful that you have wandered into it.

“In cooking, as in all the arts, simplicity is the sign of perfection.” — attributed to Apicius, De re coquinaria
