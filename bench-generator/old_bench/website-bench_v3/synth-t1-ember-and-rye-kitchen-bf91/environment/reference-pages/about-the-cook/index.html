Darlene Forthwright

Sheridan, Wyoming

Darlene grew up the youngest of four on a cattle ranch outside Sheridan, Wyoming, where Sunday suppers were a serious business and the cast-iron rotation outnumbered the dinner plates. After a decade on the line at a slow-burning steakhouse in Bozeman — and a brief, instructive detour through a Basque country bistro — she came home to document the recipes her mother and grandmother cooked without ever writing down.

Her philosophy is unfashionably patient. Good braising, she'll tell you, is mostly the art of leaving things alone: low heat, honest ingredients, and a kitchen window cracked open to let the smoke out and the afternoon in. She believes a short rib should be coaxed, never hurried, and that a glaze of rye whiskey ought to taste like the bottle it came from. There is no shortcut here she has not already tried and quietly rejected.

If a step puzzles you, a substitution worries you, or your braise simply refuses to behave, write in. Darlene answers every letter herself, usually with a pencil, often with a recipe in the margin.
