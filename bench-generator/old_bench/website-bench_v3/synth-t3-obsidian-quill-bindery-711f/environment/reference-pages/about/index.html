Our Story

A bindery built
on patience & paper.

From a Portland workshop, since 2011

Obsidian Quill Bindery was founded in 2011 by bookbinder Saoirse Nakamura in a north Portland carriage house, after a decade of apprenticeship in Kyoto and a single stubborn idea: that the materials a binder reaches for should be as considered as the books themselves.

What began as a private suppliers list — shared among friends from the Pacific Northwest Book Arts Guild — has grown into a quietly stocked shop of leathers, threads, boards, and tools used by binders, conservators, and paper artists in studios from Reykjavík to Wellington.

Ethically sourced leathers

Every hide we carry is a byproduct of food production and is traceable to a named tannery. We work primarily with two family-run tanneries in Tuscany and one in Devon, both vegetable-tanning to traditions older than the books they will eventually cover. We never stock chrome-tanned skins, and we publish the tannage, origin, and lot number on every hide page in the shop.

Traditional sewing-frame technique

Our hand-bound journals are sewn on a standing oak frame, using linen tapes and a kettle stitch — the same architecture used for European trade bindings since the sixteenth century. We round and back by hand, line the spines with airplane linen, and case in with wheat-starch paste. Each journal carries the binder's initials and the month it left the bench.

Visit the workshop

Our studio on N. Mississippi Avenue is open by appointment, Tuesday through Saturday. We're glad to pull hides for inspection, demonstrate a sewing setup, or simply share a pot of hojicha while you flip through endpaper swatches. Wholesale binders and educators are warmly invited.

— Saoirse Nakamura, founder & head binder

What we keep in mind

I

Provenance over price

We name every tannery, mill, and forge we buy from. If we can't, we don't stock it.

II

Made to be used

Materials are tested at the bench before they hit a product page — by binders, not marketers.

III

The long shelf life

We favor reversible, archival methods. A book bound today should still open in 2125.
