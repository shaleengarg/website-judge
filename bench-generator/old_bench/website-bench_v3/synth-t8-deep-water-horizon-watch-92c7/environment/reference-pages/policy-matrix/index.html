Policy Matrix · Jurisdictional Analysis

Where the Law Ends, the Net Begins

A comparative audit of the four governing frameworks tasked with policing the South Coral Sea — and three concrete reforms that would close the enforcement gap our reporting exposed.

Band 01 · Jurisdiction Cards

Four Frameworks, One Ocean

Framework · Treaty

UNCLOS

Non-Compliant

The 1982 Convention establishes high-seas conservation duties but lacks an enforcement organ. Of 11 implicated flag states, eight have ratified UNCLOS yet none have been censured by treaty bodies during the five-year window.

Framework · Regional Commission

WCPFC

Partial

The Western and Central Pacific Fisheries Commission maintains a vessel registry and observer scheme. Our cross-referencing found 247 incident reports filed but only 19 escalated to compliance review.

Framework · National Agency

Australian AFMA

Non-Compliant

The Australian Fisheries Management Authority holds clear jurisdiction over the eastern half of the survey zone. Patrol assets were reduced 38% from 2020–2023, coinciding with the steepest rise in incursions we documented.

Framework · National Agency

Papua New Guinea NFA

Partial

The National Fisheries Authority initiated three of the four successful prosecutions on record. Capacity constraints — two operational patrol craft for 2.4 million km² of EEZ — limit consistent enforcement beyond 60 nautical miles.

Band 03 · Recommended Reforms

Three Reforms That Would Close the Gap

01
Reform · Transparency

Mandatory satellite identification for every vessel above twenty-four metres operating in regulated waters.

Our reporting confirmed that 71% of identified incursions involved vessels operating with their Automatic Identification System disabled or spoofed. Existing WCPFC rules require AIS but lack a corresponding compliance mechanism: flag states self-certify, and verification is sporadic. A binding protocol — one that treats AIS-dark transits in regulated zones as prima facie evidence of unlawful fishing — would shift the burden of proof onto operators rather than understaffed national agencies.

Implementation would require modest investment in coastal receiver stations and a centralised registry administered by the WCPFC Secretariat. Three Pacific island nations have already drafted enabling legislation; what remains is political will at the Commission level. Without this baseline, every other reform in this matrix is downstream of a problem no one can reliably see. Visibility, in other words, is not a technocratic detail — it is the precondition for accountability.

02
Reform · Capacity

A regional patrol-sharing compact among Australia, Papua New Guinea, and the Solomon Islands.

No single nation in the South Coral Sea region commands enough offshore capability to consistently police its own exclusive economic zone, let alone the contested margins where most documented incidents occurred. A formal patrol-sharing compact — modelled on existing Pacific Quadrilateral Defence Coordination Group arrangements but redirected toward fisheries enforcement — would pool vessels, aircraft, and intelligence under rotating command.

"Enforcement isn't a sovereignty question. It's a logistics question that has been allowed to masquerade as one." — former AFMA patrol commander, interviewed November 2024

Critics will argue that such a compact dilutes national prerogative. The counterargument, drawn from our five-year dataset, is that uncoordinated action has produced three prosecutions against an estimated 847 violating vessels. Whatever sovereignty is being preserved is being preserved largely on paper.

03
Reform · Consequence

Port-state denial protocols binding on all signatory nations of the FAO Port State Measures Agreement.

The single most effective documented deterrent in our case files was not a patrol intercept but a refused port. When the MV Shenlong 7 was denied entry at Lae in February 2023, its operator dissolved within six months and three associated vessels relisted under new flags — a costly, traceable disruption. Yet port-state denials remain rare because individual harbour authorities lack consistent access to vessel histories, and operators exploit the gaps between jurisdictions.

A binding protocol under the FAO Port State Measures Agreement would mandate cross-jurisdictional information sharing within twenty-four hours of any compliance flag, and would require signatory ports to deny entry to any vessel under active investigation by a regional fisheries management organisation. The legal scaffolding already exists; what is missing is the obligation to use it. Closing this loop converts every commercial port into an enforcement node — a structural shift that no patrol fleet, however well-resourced, could substitute for.
