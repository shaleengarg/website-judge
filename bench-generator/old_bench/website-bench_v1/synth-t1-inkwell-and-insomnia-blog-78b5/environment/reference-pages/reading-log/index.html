Inkwell & Insomnia

Written between midnight and 4 a.m.

Reading Log, 2024

I read mostly at night, slowly, with a pencil in hand and an unreasonable number of sticky tabs. I log what I finish here partly to keep myself honest — a private list quickly becomes a list of books I meant to read — and partly because friends keep asking what's worth their time. Ratings are blunt on purpose. The one-sentence verdicts are the part I actually believe.

  1. Annie Ernaux, The Years

    A whole century compressed into a single, restless first-person plural — I underlined nearly every other line.

    5 out of 5

  2. Tsitsi Dangarembga, Nervous Conditions

    A reread that hit harder this time, probably because I've now taught girls who sound exactly like Tambu.

    5 out of 5

  3. Italo Calvino, Invisible Cities

    Better at 2 a.m. than at noon; treats prose like architecture and gets away with it.

    5 out of 5

  4. Jenny Offill, Dept. of Speculation

    Fragmentary in a way that feels like flipping through someone's marginalia rather than reading their novel.

    4 out of 5

  5. Teju Cole, Open City

    The walking is the plot; I trusted the narrator less by the end, which I think is the point.

    4 out of 5

  6. Maryse Condé, Crossing the Mangrove

    A village told through twenty mouths — quiet, communal, and surprisingly funny in the margins.

    4 out of 5

  7. Olga Tokarczuk, Flights

    Beautiful in pieces; I'm not yet convinced the pieces add up, but I keep thinking about them anyway.

    4 out of 5

  8. Ayọ̀bámi Adébáyọ̀, A Spell of Good Things

    A domestic novel that turns political without ever raising its voice — a hard trick, well done.

    4 out of 5

  9. Han Kang, The White Book

    More prose poem than novel; small, cold, and exactly the right length.

    3 out of 5

  10. Kazuo Ishiguro, Klara and the Sun

    Admirably restrained, but I wanted the novel to risk something at the moments it chose to look away.

    3 out of 5
