Field Testimony · 06 Profiles

Voices the rebuild forgot.

Between 2009 and 2024, our reporters recorded over two hundred hours of survivor testimony across six Pacific Rim communities. Six excerpts are reproduced here verbatim, translated where necessary. Names are used with consent; ages are given as of interview.

PORTRAIT · 01
Rahima Sutrisno
Padang · Indonesia · 2009

My husband was a mason. He had told me, only the week before, that the new school had been built on river silt. I did not understand what he meant until I was clawing at concrete with my hands and finding only my daughter’s shoes.

AGE 47  //  INTERVIEW 14 MIN
PORTRAIT · 02
Tomas Aguinaldo
Batangas · Philippines · 2017

The drills at our barangay hall always ended in laughter. We thought of earthquakes as something that happened in the movies, or in Japan. When the Manila Trench finally moved beneath our boat, I had thirty seconds to remember everything I had ignored.

AGE 61  //  INTERVIEW 22 MIN
PORTRAIT · 03
Linda Vargas
Seaside · Oregon, USA

We have lived here for three generations. The tsunami evacuation sign is bolted to the side of a motel that, according to the state geologist, will be underwater in eighteen minutes. Nobody on this street has a go-bag. Nobody. I have asked.

AGE 38  //  INTERVIEW 09 MIN
PORTRAIT · 04
Hemi Whaitiri
Gisborne · Aotearoa NZ

My grandfather used to say the Hikurangi was sleeping. He did not say peacefully. After the 2016 Kaikōura event we found cracks in our marae — cracks no one could explain, two hundred kilometres from the epicentre. He stopped saying sleeping after that.

AGE 54  //  INTERVIEW 31 MIN
PORTRAIT · 05
Farida Baloch
Gwadar · Makran Coast

The 1945 wave is in our oral histories, but not in our building codes. My uncle — an engineer — told the municipal council in 2019 that the new port terminal would not survive a repeat. They thanked him for his interest and approved the design unchanged.

AGE 42  //  INTERVIEW 18 MIN
PORTRAIT · 06
Kenji Morisawa
Sendai · Japan · 2011

I survived Tōhoku because of a drill I had performed forty times. Forty. I tell my colleagues in Manila and Lima this number, and I see in their faces that it sounds absurd. It is not absurd. It is the minimum. It is the cost of living near a trench.

AGE 58  //  INTERVIEW 47 MIN
