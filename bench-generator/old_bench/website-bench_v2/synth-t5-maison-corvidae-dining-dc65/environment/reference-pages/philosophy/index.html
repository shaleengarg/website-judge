A Philosophy of the Single Ingredient

Céleste Morin was raised in the kitchens of Lyon, in the narrow apartment above her grandmother's charcuterie on the rue Mercière, where the morning began with the sound of a knife against a board and the smell of thyme drying in the window. She learned, before she could read, that a meal is an argument made in favor of a particular hour of a particular day — that a tomato in August is not the same proposition as a tomato in March, and ought never be asked to behave as one. This conviction, gently planted, would become the architecture of everything that followed.

At seventeen she left for Paris, where she apprenticed under Chef Henri Vaugirard at his two-Michelin-starred Le Cormoran, and then, for three formative winters, under the late Madame Solange Bréchet, whose kitchen on the rue de Sèvres operated on the principle that restraint is the highest form of generosity. From Vaugirard she took precision; from Bréchet, patience. From both she took the understanding that technique is in service of memory, never the reverse.

We do not cook to impress. We cook to remember. — Céleste Morin, Founder

Maison Corvidae was opened in the autumn of 2017 in a restored 1920s townhouse on NW Kearney Street, twenty-eight seats arranged around a single seven-course service that begins, every evening, at half past seven. The guiding rule of the house is simple, and inflexible: every dish must honor a single seasonal ingredient above all others. The remainder of the plate exists to frame it, to translate it, to give it the dignity of a clear sentence. Nothing competes; nothing crowds.

The name belongs to the corvids — the crows, the magpies, the ravens — birds that gather what is rare, remember what is hidden, and return to the same branches year after year. It is, we think, an honest description of how a kitchen ought to behave.
