Our Work

Across the Ozark plateau, Hollow Creek Rewilding focuses on three interlocking programs that restore native ecosystems while building lasting capacity within the communities who steward them. Our approach is patient, regional, and grounded in the conviction that healthy landscapes are inseparable from healthy neighborhoods.

Riparian Buffer Planting is our oldest initiative, running continuously since 2014 along the Finley Creek tributaries that thread through working farmland in three counties. Each spring our crews install deep-rooted sedges, river cane, swamp white oak, and buttonbush along eroding banks, stabilizing soils that had been losing more than an inch a year. Buffers cool the water, filter agricultural runoff, and create movement corridors for box turtles, otters, and migratory songbirds returning to floodplain habitat that had largely vanished after mid-century channelization.

The Ozark Prairie Seed Library is the quiet engine of nearly everything else we do. Housed in a climate-controlled vault on loan from the regional agricultural extension, the library now safeguards 94 native species harvested by volunteers from remnant prairies, glades, and roadside refugia across the plateau. Genetic provenance is tracked meticulously: every accession is labeled by drainage and ecoregion so that restorations carry forward locally adapted populations rather than commercial cultivars. Seed is shared at no cost with partner restoration projects, schools, and neighbors.

Schoolyard Habitat Grants put that seed, and the ecological knowledge behind it, into the hands of the next generation of stewards. We currently partner with 12 local schools — from a two-room rural elementary in Stone County to the largest middle school in Springfield — funding pollinator meadows, rain gardens, and outdoor classrooms designed and maintained by students. Teachers receive curriculum support, and graduating cohorts of student-naturalists join our adult volunteer crews each summer, carrying the work forward.
