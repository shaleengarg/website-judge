The Cartographer's Elegy · Poem 07 of 18

Surveying the Flood Plain at Dusk

by Mirela Okonkwo

When the river withdrew it left a map of itself —

silt the color of bruised pears, the shape

of every promise the season had broken,

and the tin smell of something gone home.

I walked the perimeter with my father's compass

and a notebook he had not yet died to give me.

The needle wandered. The needle had its reasons.

I followed it past the willow, past the fence-post

that had become a heron in the soft light,

or perhaps had always been one, waiting

for me to learn the difference between

what is rooted and what is only patient.

There are charts in the county office

that name this acre by its absence of water.

There are charts in my body that disagree.

My grandmother surveyed by listening —

she said grief is the only honest cartographer,

that it draws the country as it actually is,

not as the road wishes, not as the deed insists,

but as the rain remembers, season after season.

I have named every inlet

the water has already forgotten.

I have stood at the meridian of my own small grief

and watched the moths arrive like minor saints,

their wings the color of paper left in weather.

Dusk is a kind of surveying. The plain

folds its edges inward. I write the field's last name

in pencil, in case the river comes back to claim it.

I have named every inlet / the water has already forgotten.
