Mapped Territory  ·  Part II of V

A Geography
of Loss.

Six neighborhoods inside the old Harwick municipal line. Each name predates the 1973 closure; each one means something different after it. What follows is a survey of streets that still appear on the township plat, and the absences they now contain.

Field Survey  ·  Six Locations

The Named Places

Cards compiled from the 1968 Harwick borough directory cross-referenced against 1981 county assessor revaluations. Coordinates are approximate.

Location 01

Slag Hill

An artificial ridge built from sixty years of cooled byproduct, rising forty feet above Allegheny River Road. Before 1973 it was a workplace — night-crews dumped from rail-cars under sodium lamps. After, it became a place where teenagers parked, a place that nothing legal would grow on, and finally a Superfund line item filed in 1987.

40.5341° N / 79.8127° W
Location 02

Coke Row

Forty-eight identical company duplexes built in 1919 along a single unpaved lane behind the coking ovens. Before closure: rented at $42/month to married laborers with seniority. After: condemned in two waves, 1976 and 1981; eleven units squatted into the mid-eighties; the lane officially de-listed from the township map in 1990.

40.5359° N / 79.8094° W
Location 03

The Flats

The low ground between the rolling mill and the river — six blocks of tavern, grocer, barber, union hall, two churches. Before 1973 it ran a three-shift economy. After, the storefronts emptied in a documented eighteen-month cascade; by 1979 only Stachowski's tavern and a single Polish bakery remained operating past nightfall.

40.5328° N / 79.8141° W
Location 04

Mill Gate

The east approach to the works — a wrought-iron arch dated 1903, a guard shack, and the asphalt apron where shift-change happened. Before closure: four thousand bodies crossed it twice a day. After: the arch stood another six years, was sold for scrap in 1979, and now exists only in a photograph held by the Allegheny County Historical Society.

40.5344° N / 79.8109° W
Location 05

Furnace Bend

The wide curve where the river turned south past Number Three blast furnace. Before 1973 it was the loudest place in the county — a sound residents claimed they could feel through their molars. After, the silence was the first thing visiting reporters wrote about. The furnace itself was demolished in 1984 over the course of nine working days.

40.5316° N / 79.8158° W
Location 06

Widow's Walk

A pedestrian bridge over the rail spur, named in the 1940s for the wives who waited there for end-of-shift whistles. Before 1973 it was a daily transit point. After, it became a memorial without ever being formally designated one — flowers tied to the railings each March 14th, the anniversary of the final pour, continuing into the 2000s.

40.5352° N / 79.8083° W

Displacement  ·  A Reading

The places stay named. The people do not stay.

Cartography is the discipline of insisting that things have edges. Harwick's borough surveyors, returning each spring from 1903 onward, walked the same fence-lines and recorded the same boundaries — Slag Hill ended where the rail-bed began; Coke Row was forty-eight numbered lots; the Flats had two churches, both Catholic, both on the south side of Vine Street. The map did not change after March of 1973. What changed was the relationship between the line on the page and the population it was meant to enclose.

Within eighteen months of the final pour, twelve hundred residents had left the borough. Within five years, the number had doubled. The county assessor's office, working with a methodology designed to track property rather than persons, recorded only the consequences: vacant parcels, delinquent taxes, the slow downward revaluation of houses that no one would buy at the prices their owners needed.

"You can map a town. You cannot map the leaving of one — only the holes it leaves in the assessor's book."

What the index opposite represents is an attempt — incomplete, partial, compiled from union pension records, parish registries, and three rounds of oral-history interviews conducted between 1998 and 2014 — to attach names to the disappearance. Each entry is a household, not a person; each decade is a window of departure, not a date. The list is not exhaustive. It is not meant to be. It is meant to register the texture of a leaving that the standard cartographies — economic, statistical, federal — were not equipped to see.

Some names recur. The Stachowskis stayed; three branches of the Fenwick family did not. The Aurelio Betts household split between Pittsburgh and Lorain, Ohio, within the same calendar year. Vera Mack Cullen, whose testimony anchors the next section of this essay, kept her Furnace Bend address until 2009 — among the last in her block.
