A Brief Account of This Newspaper

On the origins, the obligations, and the stubborn convictions of The Western Tribune, in its one hundred and forty-second year.

The Western Tribune was founded in the autumn of 1882 in a single rented room above a feed store, by three printers who believed that the towns of the high plateau deserved a paper of their own. The first edition ran to four pages, was set entirely by hand, and sold for three cents. It contained a livestock report, two editorials on the question of statehood, an account of a wedding, and a long, errant poem submitted by the eldest founder's sister-in-law. The poem, by most accounts, did not improve the paper, but it did establish a tradition of generosity toward unsolicited contributions that has, in fits and starts, survived to the present day.

In the century and a half since, the Tribune has changed almost everything about how it works — its presses, its typefaces, its distribution, its ownership, and at certain difficult moments its name — while attempting to change as little as possible about what it is for. We exist to report, with care and at length, on the places and questions that other publications find too remote, too slow, or too unprofitable to cover well. We are skeptical of speed for its own sake. We believe a story held back for a day, and corrected, is worth ten stories rushed to print and quietly amended.

The newsroom today employs forty-three reporters and editors, working from bureaus in five cities and a great many spare bedrooms. Our coverage is organized around four standing desks — Politics, Culture, Technology, and a general assignment desk that handles everything else, which is most of the news — and a rotating roster of contributors whose interests are too particular to fit any desk at all. We are owned by a nonprofit foundation established in 1994, which has the singular advantage of relieving us from the obligation to grow forever, and the singular disadvantage of requiring us to ask, every year, for your support.

What we promise, in exchange, is the work itself: careful, slow, and occasionally beautiful. We will be wrong sometimes, and when we are, we will say so plainly and on the same page where the error first appeared. We will not chase a story we do not believe, and we will not abandon one merely because the rest of the press has moved on. We will continue to publish poems, when they are good, and even, on rare occasions, when they are not. We are grateful, every Friday morning, that you have chosen to read us.

— The Editors
