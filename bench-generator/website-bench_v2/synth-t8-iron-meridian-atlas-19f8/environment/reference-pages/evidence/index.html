Analysis

What the documents and the testimonies cannot both be telling the truth about.

Set Memo M-008 beside Dolores Fenwick's 1986 interview and the page begins to vibrate. In May 1973, the Plant Manager posted a notice at every gate calling rumors of closure "unfounded and harmful to morale." Fenwick — the clerk who had been instructed to type the closure schedule the previous January — was the one operating the mimeograph. Two artifacts, one room, six months of overlap, and a single irreducible contradiction.

This is the work the archive performs. It does not adjudicate; it juxtaposes. The memos read as the prose of a coherent institution making rational decisions under fiduciary pressure. The testimonies read as the prose of a coherent community making rational decisions under the assumption that they had been told the truth. Both cannot have been operating in the same room in the same months in the same town. One of them was being lied to.

The 1973 closure of Harwick Steel Works was not, as Meridian Industrial Holdings would later characterize it in their 1976 annual report, an unfortunate response to "rapidly evolving market conditions." Memos M-002 and M-004 demonstrate that strategic divestiture was commissioned fifteen months before the public announcement, by a firm whose deliverable was scheduled before any of the conditions cited in Meridian's later statements had materialized. The conditions were the alibi. The decision was the premise.

What remains, after the documents and the voices have been placed in the same room, is a small and specific charge: that the men and women of Harwick were owed the dignity of being told the truth in time to plan around it, and that they were systematically denied that dignity by people who knew, who had written it down, and who had filed those writings in a locked steel cabinet. The cabinet was opened in 1991. The page you have just read is part of what fell out.
