How Sedge Grass Returned to Miller's Bend

By Marcus Whitfield — Spring 2024

For nine years the floodplain behind Miller's Bend held only fescue and burdock. We seeded path rush and Carex frankii along the channel last March, and by July the banks were knit tight. Otters showed up in October. I'd been told sedge wouldn't take this far west of the Buffalo—I'm grateful to have been wrong about that.

The Year the Bobwhites Came Home

By Eleanor Ramos — Autumn 2023

My grandfather used to count quail coveys on his morning walk. By the time he passed in 2009, the whistling had gone quiet. After three seasons of little bluestem plantings on the south forty, I heard the first 'bob-WHITE' last August at dawn. I cried into my coffee. Then I called my mother to tell her.

Pulling Bush Honeysuckle in November Rain

By Jamie Okafor — Late Fall 2023

There is nothing romantic about invasive removal. It is cold, wet, and your hands ache. But when you uproot a fifteen-foot honeysuckle stand and see the pawpaw seedlings underneath that have been waiting a decade for sunlight—that is the closest thing to faith I've felt. We cleared two acres on the Finley tributary that weekend.

Seed Harvest at Pawnee Ridge

By Theo Linwood — Summer 2024

Eighteen of us spent four Saturdays gathering compass plant, rattlesnake master, and prairie blazing star from a remnant ridge slated for development. We logged 11 pounds of clean seed for the Ozark Prairie Seed Library. The landowner cried when she handed over the key. The bulldozers came that Monday. The seeds outlived the field.

Teaching Third Graders to Read a Watershed

By Priya Anand — Spring 2024

Mrs. Calloway's class adopted a hundred-yard stretch of Finley Creek as part of our Schoolyard Habitat Grant. We measured turbidity, identified caddisfly larvae, and planted buttonbush along the cutbank. By May, every kid could name three indicator species. One girl told me she wants to be a 'creek doctor' when she grows up. I told her that was a real job.

The Burn That Brought Back the Coneflowers

By Russell Tate — Spring 2023

Prescribed fire scares people, and I understand why. But the prairie evolved with it. We torched twelve acres of degraded pasture last April under a green crew supervisor's watch. Six weeks later, pale purple coneflowers I'd never seen on that land emerged from a dormant seed bank decades old. The fire didn't destroy anything. It remembered.
