About · The Writer

Maren Voss

A dim window, a kettle, a desk lamp at 2:14 a.m.

For eleven years I was a municipal librarian in Akureyri, in a small stone building three blocks from the harbor, where the heating pipes ticked through every winter afternoon and the children's section smelled faintly of wet wool. I learned, in that building, that quiet is not the absence of sound but a particular kind of attention.

I now live in Reykjavik, in a top-floor flat with one good chair and too many books, and I write for a living — mostly essays, occasionally reviews, and these journal entries, which I keep because I cannot sleep and would rather not waste the dark. Most of what you read here was first drafted between midnight and 3 a.m., when the city is at its most honest.

My subjects tend to be small: a recipe card from a stranger, the layout of a closed branch library, the way a particular street looks when the snow has been on it for a week. I am interested in memory, in place, and in the slow, ordinary work of paying attention to the ordinary.

I do not have social media. I answer email slowly. I read everything that is sent to me, eventually, and I am grateful for it — especially the long, rambling letters that arrive on weekday mornings when I have just gone to bed.

If you would like to know what I am reading this year, the reading list is updated whenever I finish (or abandon) a book. If you would like to know what I have been thinking about for longer than an evening, the essays are the better place.
