Chapter 05 — Policy & Action

The Earthquakes Nobody Is Preparing For Will Not Wait for Consensus.

Five reforms separate the cities that survive a great subduction-zone rupture from those that do not. None of them are technically difficult. All of them are politically inconvenient.

Five Reforms That Cannot Wait

01Mandate Seismic Retrofit Deadlines, Not Suggestions

Voluntary retrofit programs have, across thirty years of Pacific Rim policy, retrofitted approximately twelve percent of high-risk unreinforced masonry stock. Hard deadlines — with permit and insurance consequences — are the only mechanism that has ever moved that number above twenty.

Oregon's 2005 voluntary list of 1,640 vulnerable schools still contained 1,189 of those same buildings in 2024. The deadline is the lever; everything else is rhetoric.

02Fund Multi-Channel Early-Warning to the Last Mile

ShakeAlert and Japan's J-Alert demonstrate that 60 to 90 seconds of warning is achievable. Coverage gaps remain in coastal Indonesia, the Philippines, and the Makran. The cost per protected resident is below US$0.40 per year — less than a postage stamp against a forty-second window of survival.

"A warning that never reaches a phone, a school bell, or a hospital PA system is not a warning. It is a press release." — Dr. M. Adisa, GNS

03Codify Tsunami Vertical Evacuation Structures

Seaside, Oregon has one. Padang has three. The Sendai coastline, after 2011, built eighty-seven. Vertical evacuation is the only viable strategy for inundation zones where horizontal escape exceeds the tsunami arrival window — and the building code language to require them already exists in draft.

04Require Annual, Auditable Public Drills

Drill participation is the single strongest predictor of post-event survival behavior in five separate longitudinal studies. Yet only Japan, Chile, and parts of New Zealand audit participation rates. The remainder report aspirational figures with no verification mechanism.

05Ring-Fence Disaster Funding from Annual Budget Cycles

Preparedness budgets are the first cut in every fiscal contraction. A statutory floor — indexed to population in mapped hazard zones — converts disaster funding from a discretionary line item into infrastructure, where it belongs.
