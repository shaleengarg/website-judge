North America · Pacific Plate Boundary

Cascadia: The Locked Megathrust.

The Cascadia Subduction Zone runs roughly 1,100 km from Cape Mendocino, California, to northern Vancouver Island, where the Juan de Fuca plate dives beneath North America at an average slip rate of 38 mm/year. Paleoseismic core samples from coastal estuaries reveal a recurrence interval of 240–500 years.

The last full-margin rupture — magnitude 9.0 — occurred on 26 January 1700, dated precisely by the orphan tsunami logged in Edo-period Japanese village records. More than three centuries of accumulated strain now rest beneath Seattle, Portland, and Eureka, with FEMA modeling estimating 14,000 fatalities in a worst-case rupture scenario.

Length 1,100 km
Slip 38 mm/yr
Last Rupture 1700
New Zealand · Australian–Pacific Boundary

Hikurangi: Slow-Slip and Sudden Release.

Stretching ~3,500 km of trench system from East Cape down to the Marlborough faults, the Hikurangi margin accommodates Pacific Plate convergence at 40–45 mm/year. GNS Science has documented repeat slow-slip events along the northern Hikurangi at 18-month intervals — silent earthquakes that release energy without seismic radiation.

The southern segment, however, is fully locked. No major rupture has been geologically confirmed since approximately AD 1450, leaving Wellington exposed to a plausible M8.9 event. Recent seabed cores reveal turbidite layers consistent with a 500-year recurrence window now closing.

Length 3,500 km
Slip 42 mm/yr
Last Rupture ~1450

Philippines · South China Sea Margin

Manila Trench: The Quiet Gap.

The Manila Trench extends 1,200 km from southern Taiwan to Mindoro, where the South China Sea plate subducts eastward beneath the Luzon volcanic arc at 50–80 mm/year — among the fastest convergence rates in the Pacific Rim. PHIVOLCS designates the central segment a Class A seismic gap.

Despite the rate, no M≥8.0 has been instrumentally recorded along the trench. Tsunami modeling for a hypothetical M9.0 event predicts wave heights of 8–11 metres reaching Hong Kong's outer islands within 90 minutes. Manila's 14 million residents remain largely outside coastal evacuation infrastructure.

Length 1,200 km
Slip 70 mm/yr
Last Rupture None inst.
Pakistan & Iran · Arabian Plate Boundary

Makran: The Forgotten Megathrust.

Often excluded from "Pacific Rim" inventories despite its identical subduction mechanics, the Makran zone extends ~900 km along the southern coasts of Iran and Pakistan, where the Arabian Plate descends beneath Eurasia at a comparatively slow 19 mm/year. The unusually shallow subduction angle produces a wide, low-relief accretionary wedge.

The 1945 Balochistan earthquake (M8.1) triggered a tsunami that killed an estimated 4,000 people along the coasts of present-day Pakistan, Iran, Oman, and India. Recurrence modeling suggests 125–250 year intervals; the eastern segment remains unruptured since 1765.

Length 900 km
Slip 19 mm/yr
Last Rupture 1945
