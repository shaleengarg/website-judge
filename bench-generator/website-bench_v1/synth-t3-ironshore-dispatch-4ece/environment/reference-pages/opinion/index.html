Opinion · Foreign Policy

The Quiet Unraveling of the Postwar Compact

By Eleanor Marsh · October 16, 2024

For seventy years, the architecture of the postwar order rested on a comfortable fiction: that the powers who built it would also be the powers who maintained it. The fiction was useful, because it allowed smaller states to plan, to trade, and to imagine themselves participants in a system whose rules were broadly legible. That legibility is now gone, replaced by a thicket of bilateral deals, security exceptions, and ad hoc coalitions that no one bothers to name.

The shift did not arrive as a single rupture. It came piecemeal — a tariff here, a sanctions package there, a security guarantee withdrawn without announcement. Diplomats in Berlin and Seoul tell me, off the record, that the cumulative effect has been to make every meeting smaller, every deliverable narrower, and every promise contingent on the next election cycle in a capital several time zones away.

"The rules-based order did not die. It was quietly downgraded to a rules-influenced order, and then to a rules-considered one."

What replaces the old compact is not chaos, exactly, but a kind of permanent improvisation. States that once relied on multilateral forums now hedge: they keep a seat at the table while building parallel arrangements with whichever partner seems most reliable that quarter. The energy this consumes is enormous, and the bandwidth lost to hedging is bandwidth that used to go to actual problem-solving.

The question facing the next generation of foreign ministers is not whether to defend the old order — that battle, on the available evidence, is over — but whether anything coherent can be assembled in its place before the improvisation hardens into something less benign. The answer will depend on whether the middle powers can find common cause faster than the great powers can divide them. The early signs are not encouraging.

Opinion · Technology

We Are Outsourcing Judgment, and We Have Not Noticed

By Priya Rangan · October 15, 2024

The most consequential technology story of the year is not a product launch. It is the steady, almost invisible migration of decisions — about credit, hiring, housing, sentencing, and now medical triage — from human deliberation into systems that nobody in the room fully understands. The migration is rarely announced. It is implemented as an efficiency, and audited, if at all, by the same vendor that sold it.

I have spent the last eight months sitting in procurement meetings at municipal agencies across three countries. The pattern is consistent: the technical staff describe the model in cautious, hedged language; the executives hear "decision support"; the line workers hear "do what the screen says." By the time a citizen is on the receiving end, the chain of accountability has gone through four contractors and a confidentiality clause.

Defenders of these systems argue, fairly, that human judgment is also flawed, biased, and inconsistent. This is true. But human judgment can be questioned in a hallway, appealed in a hearing, and overruled by a supervisor who has read the file. Algorithmic judgment, deployed at scale, tends to harden into infrastructure. Once a model is woven into a workflow, the cost of removing it becomes the argument for keeping it.

The remedy is not to ban the tools — that ship sailed during the last budget cycle — but to insist, in law and in contract, that any system that makes consequential decisions about a person be legible to that person, contestable in a forum that actually meets, and replaceable on a timeline shorter than a generation. We are not there. We are not close. And we are running out of decisions we still get to make by hand.
