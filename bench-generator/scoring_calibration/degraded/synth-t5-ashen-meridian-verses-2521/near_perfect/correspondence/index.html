Correspondence

Three Letters

Two received, one withheld. A small archive kept beside the manuscript.

Letter I

To the Uncle, Unreplied

Solenne Vray → Henri Vray (d. 2009)

Drafted Strasbourg, November 2022

Henri — I have been writing the foundry again. The one at the end of rue Albert‑Schweitzer, the long shed with the broken transom you used to point at from the bridge as though pointing were a kind of authorship. I keep finding your hands inside the poems. They appear at the margins, smelling of grease and tobacco, doing the small impossible labor of holding things in place while I name them.

I was eight when you taught me the word tréfilerie. I did not know then that the word would outlive the place. The shed is now a parking lot for a discount supermarket. The transom is in someone's garden in Belfort, I am told, leaning against a wall like a saint who has lost his church.

Mother says you would not have liked the book. She says the poems are too soft for the work they describe, that you would have wanted iron and instead I have given you weather. Perhaps. But weather is what the iron eventually becomes. I am trying to write the long after.

I am sending this letter to the address that no longer exists, which is to say I am sending it nowhere, which is to say I am sending it to you.

Ever your niece,
Solenne

Letter II

From a Reader in Strasbourg

Marguerite C. → Solenne Vray

Received March 2024, postmarked Strasbourg

Madame Vray,

I am sixty‑three and I have not written to a poet before. I am writing because of the small green book a friend pressed into my hands at the Librairie Kléber, which I read on a single train from Strasbourg to Sélestat and then read again, slowly, the same evening, with a glass of wine I forgot to drink.

My father worked the rolling mill at Hagondange. He came home grey, the grey of a thing that has surrendered its color to a more demanding palette. When he died there was a long argument among his sons about what to do with his work jacket. I am the only daughter and was not consulted. I think of him when I read your Foundry, 1973. I think he would not have understood the poem and would have been moved by it anyway, the way he was moved by music in languages he could not place.

Your Slag Heap Elegy made me sit down in the street. I do not know what else to say about it. I sat down on a low wall near the cathedral and read it twice and then put the book in my coat and walked home. I want you to know that this happened. I want it placed in a record somewhere.

Thank you for the book. Thank you for the difficulty of it. Thank you for refusing to make our towns into a backdrop.

Very respectfully,
Marguerite C.

"Your Slag Heap Elegy made me sit down in the street." — Marguerite C., March 2024
Letter III

Unsent Draft to an Editor

Solenne Vray → [unaddressed]

Folded inside the manuscript, undated

You have asked me to clarify the title. I will not. The phrase belongs first to a foreman whose name I have not been able to recover, who used it in a logbook in Thionville in the autumn of 1961 to describe a shift in which two of his men were injured and the furnace continued anyway. The hour that eats itself. He underlined it. I have copied the underline.

You have also asked whether the collection might be shortened. I understand the economy of the question. I will say only that seventeen is the number of poems the book required to become what it is, and that the silences between them are themselves a kind of poem, and that removing any one of them would collapse a load‑bearing wall in a building you have not been inside.

I do not mean to be difficult. I mean to be exact. There is a difference, though it is sometimes small, and I have spent ten years among industrial archives learning to honor the small differences.

If this is unworkable, I will understand. I have other readers. I have, increasingly, a quieter audience — a woman in Strasbourg, a cousin in Forbach, my uncle who is dead. I write for them now. I find the work clearer when I do.

With regard, and without apology,
S. V.
