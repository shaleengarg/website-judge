Collection

Three Poems, In Full

Selected from the collection.

A language for what the foundries could not say, and what the daughters were left holding.
II.

Slag Heap Elegy

They built a hill from what the furnace would not keep,
a black geometry rising at the edge of the commune,
and we, children of the late shift, climbed it
as if it were a saint's relic, knees grey, palms grey.

My uncle said the heap had its own weather —
a small wind that carried the smell of cooled iron
and the particular silence of a thing
that had been useful once and was no longer.

We buried the village inside its own residue.
We buried, also, the names of the men who knew
which slag was Tuesday's and which was Thursday's,
which belonged to the war and which to the peace after.

Now grass goes over it the way forgetting goes over a face,
patient, indifferent, almost tender.

III.

My Mother's Hands Are Trowels

She came home from the laundry with her fingers already shaped
for the garden — flat at the tip, calloused at the base,
a tool the body had agreed to become
so that the rest of the body could rest.

She planted absences in a row, watered nothing,
and still, by August, the absences had grown
into a kind of crop we ate without naming.

I asked her once what she missed of the old country.
She held up her hands — the trowels — and said:
I miss the soil that knew them as hands.

And the kitchen, which had been listening,
drew its curtains an inch tighter against the afternoon.

IV.

The Canal in February

Fog has come down to the towpath again
and the barges are merely the rumor of barges,
long grey shapes that the river refuses to finish
the sentence of.

A heron stands so still it has joined the architecture.
A man passes with a dog that does not lift its head.
The mill on the opposite bank
has not been a mill since the year my father stopped speaking.

I count the locks downstream — one, two, three —
the way one counts the rooms of a house
one is preparing, slowly, to leave.

February has the patience of an undertaker.
The canal carries the cold without complaint,
carries it east, toward the border, toward whatever country
still has use for our weather.
