The Hour That Eats Itself

Seventeen poems · industrial grief, inherited silence, the geography of eastern France

“Attention is the rarest and purest form of generosity. To pay attention is to suffer.” — Simone Weil

Plate I — Thionville, anonymous

I.

Foundry, 1973

after a photograph of my grandfather, third shift

The light here is not light.
It is the memory of light
held inside iron a half-second longer
than the body that poured it.
My grandfather’s hands —
I never saw them clean.
Even washed, they kept the metal
in the half-moons of the nails,
a residue more durable than name.

He came home through a door
that no longer exists,
into a kitchen whose tiles
were the color of weak tea,
and sat. He sat as if sitting
were a craft taught in apprenticeship.
My grandmother set bread on the table
without speaking. The bread also did not speak.

Outside, the foundry continued
to be a foundry. The hour
ate the hour before it,
and the men, walking the road
in their grey coveralls,
looked like nothing so much
as the photograph
a photograph would later become.

I was not born yet. I have inherited
the silence between the bread
and the chair, the way one inherits
a watch that has stopped —
knowing the time it shows
is the time something ended,
and refusing, still, to wind it.
