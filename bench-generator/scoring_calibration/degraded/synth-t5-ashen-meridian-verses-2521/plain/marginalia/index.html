The Workshop Floor

Marginalia

Working notes, abandoned stanzas, and the small repairs that never made it into the finished book. These pages are reproduced from the poet's typescripts and pocket notebooks, kept between 2017 and 2022. Crossings-out are preserved; insertions appear in rust-amber.

Draft I

Foundry, 1973 — opening lines

Notebook fragment, undated

› The men came home with their faces still burning, › their faces still burning under the soot. › The men came home with the furnace still inside them, their faces a second iron, slower to cool. › My grandmother set out four plates. › She set the plates down like coins like things she did not own. Margin: "too much weather in line 1 — cut sky, keep skin."

Draft II

Slag Heap Elegy — middle passage

Typescript, Fontevraud residency, March 2021

What the mill exhaled, the hill inhaled. What the mill exhaled was kept by the hill. The hill kept what the mill could not afford to remember. We walked the perimeter on Sundays, my uncle naming each rusted thing in the patois my mother refused my mother had been taught to mistrust. Nothing grew there for a long time. Even the thistles arrived late, and apologetic. Margin: "the elegy is for the soil, not the men. correct the pronouns."
the furnace keeps no record of what it consumed

— from a discarded stanza of Slag Heap Elegy, cut in the final revision

Variant

The Canal in February — closing couplet

Three versions, recto of the same page

a. The water held the bridge upside down, and did not complain. b. The water held the bridge upside down — a patience I have never managed. c. The water held the bridge the way déprise holds a hand: without grip, without refusal. Margin: "c. — keep. the French word does the work the English line cannot."

Draft III

My Mother's Hands Are Trowels — abandoned ending

Loose leaf, clipped to the manuscript

She planted nothing she expected to see flower. She planted things she would not live to see flower. She planted the kind of patience that does not require an audience. I inherited her wrists. I inherited the silence in her wrists. I inherited the verb she would not conjugate. Margin, in pencil: "stop trying to finish the poem. it is finished. it ends at the trowel."

A Note on the Title

The Hour That Eats Itself

The phrase is not the poet's own. It appears in the margin of a 1961 shift-foreman's logbook held in the municipal archive at Thionville, written in faded blue pencil beside an entry recording an unscheduled stoppage on the night shift of 14 November. The foreman, one M. Étienne Kuntz, wrote in the right-hand column:

l'heure qui se mange elle-même

No further explanation is given in the logbook, and Kuntz left no other writings the archive has been able to identify. Solenne Vray encountered the entry in 2018, in the course of cataloguing the Lorraine industrial papers, and copied the phrase into her notebook the same afternoon. It became the working title of the collection within a week and was never replaced.

— editor's note, Ashen Meridian edition, 2024
