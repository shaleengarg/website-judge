The Cook

Miriam Osei-Bonsu

Miriam cooks out of a narrow terraced kitchen in Bedminster, Bristol, where a battered enamel Dutch oven lives more or less permanently on the hob. She learned to braise from her grandmother in Kumasi, who insisted that any cut worth eating was worth three or four hours of patience, and who taught her to listen for the particular soft sigh a pot makes when the heat is finally right. Most of what Miriam knows about coaxing tenderness out of stubborn cuts of meat traces back to those long Saturday afternoons standing on a stool beside the stove.

In her late twenties she spent two years living in Marrakech, working part-time at a small riad and falling in love with the medina's spice merchants, the brined lemons that turn up in everything, and the slow rhythm of tagine cookery. The lamb shoulder on this site is the dish she has cooked more than any other since coming home — a quiet collision of her grandmother's braising instincts and the preserved-lemon pantry she carried back from Morocco. She is not a chef, not a food writer, just a home cook who wanted one recipe written down properly.

Philosophy

“Slow cooking isn't about waiting — it's about agreeing, finally, to be where you are while the pot does its work.”
