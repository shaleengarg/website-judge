Method

Eight steps, four hours, one quiet afternoon.

1.Bring the lamb to room temperature

Take the shoulder out of the fridge a full hour before you plan to cook. Pat it thoroughly dry with paper towel and season generously all over with flaky salt and cracked black pepper. A dry surface is the difference between a sear and a steam.

2.Sear, deeply and without rushing

Heat a heavy lidded casserole over medium-high with a slick of olive oil until it shimmers. Brown the shoulder on every face — six or seven minutes a side — until the crust is mahogany, not merely tan. Lift it onto a plate and pour off all but a tablespoon of the rendered fat.

3.Soften the aromatics

Lower the heat to medium and add the sliced onions with a pinch of salt. Cook for ten to twelve minutes, stirring occasionally, until they collapse and turn golden at the edges. Stir in the garlic, cumin, coriander and a generous grind of pepper, and let the spices bloom for thirty seconds.

4.Build the braise

Pour in the white wine and scrape the base of the pan clean with a wooden spoon. Let it reduce by half — a minute or two — then add the stock, the quartered preserved lemons and the cracked olives. The liquid should look bright and a little salty; taste it now, before the lamb goes back in.

5.Return the lamb and settle it in

Nestle the shoulder back into the pot, fat side up. The liquid should come roughly two-thirds of the way up the meat — top up with a splash more stock or water if needed. Bring it to a bare simmer on the hob before moving on.

6.Into the oven, low and slow

Cover with a tight lid and slide into an oven preheated to 160°C (325°F). Cook undisturbed for three hours, then check: the meat should yield to a spoon but still hold its shape. If it resists, give it another twenty to thirty minutes.

7.Uncover and finish

Lift the lid for the final thirty minutes of cooking so the top of the shoulder burnishes and the sauce thickens slightly. You're looking for the surface to glaze and for the braising liquid to coat the back of a spoon. Skim any pooled fat from the surface before serving.

8.Rest, then pull at the table

Let the lamb rest in its pot, lid ajar, for at least twenty minutes — this is not optional. Scatter the gremolata over the top just before bringing it to the table, and pull the meat apart with two forks in front of your guests. Spoon the bright, lemony juices over everything.
