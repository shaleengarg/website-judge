Une Table,
Deux Villes

Chapter One

Élise Fournier was nineteen the winter she first pushed open the steamed-glass door of a bouchon on rue Mercière, hands raw with November and a duffel bag of knives slung across her shoulder. She had come to Lyon from a farm outside Trois-Rivières on the strength of a single letter—four lines from a chef who had eaten her grandmother's tourtière and recognized, somewhere in its lard and clove, the patience of a serious cook. For seven years she stayed. She learned to portion a quenelle with two soup spoons until her wrists ached, to coax marrow from veal bones at four in the morning, to argue in market French about the colour of a chicken's foot.

The bouchon was not romantic. It was loud, the floors were always slightly wet, and the chef shouted in a dialect she did not understand for nearly a year. But the dining room—stone-walled, low-ceilinged, lit by candles set into wax-glazed bottles—was the most honest place she had ever stood. Regulars sat at the same tables for twenty years. The menu changed when the weather did. Nothing was ever plated to be photographed; everything was plated to be eaten, immediately, while the cook watched from the pass.

"The bouchon taught me that a great meal is an act of stubbornness against convenience."

She came home in the spring of 2011, with a husband she had met over a shared cigarette behind the walk-in and a half-formed idea of a room in Montreal that might feel, on a Tuesday in February, the way Lyon had felt on a Tuesday in February. The Plateau was less obvious then—rents were climbing but the storefronts on Saint-Denis still held quiet trades: a luthier, a milliner, a bookseller who closed for two hours at lunch. Between a florist and that bookshop she found a narrow black door with no sign, behind it a stone-walled room with a single working fireplace. Maison Cendrée opened that October, with thirty-two seats and a menu written each morning in pencil. It has been written in pencil every morning since.
