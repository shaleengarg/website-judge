The Land We Protect

Hollow Creek Land Trust safeguards 14,000 acres across three distinct Appalachian landscapes: ridge-top hardwood forest blanketing the upper slopes of the Blue Ridge, riparian wetlands threading along Hollow Creek and its tributaries, and working farmland nestled in the Crestview Valley. Each landscape requires a different stewardship approach, but together they form a connected mosaic of habitat, watershed, and rural livelihood that defines our region.

Ridge-Top Hardwood Forest

Oak, hickory, and tulip poplar canopies shelter migratory songbirds, salamanders, and dwindling stands of native chestnut sprouts. We protect more than 8,400 acres of contiguous forest along the upper ridgelines.

Riparian Wetlands

The Hollow Creek corridor filters drinking water for three downstream communities and shelters native brook trout, hellbenders, and rare bog turtles. Buffer easements extend an average of 150 feet from each bank.

Crestview Valley Farmland

Family farms in the Crestview Valley grow heritage apples, pasture-raised livestock, and small-grain crops on protected soils. Agricultural easements keep working land working while restricting subdivision and development.
