Tips & Notes

Small refinements that make the difference between a good braise and a memorable one.

Choosing Your Cut

Ask the butcher for English-style, bone-in short ribs cut roughly two inches thick. The bone is non-negotiable — it lends body and gloss to the braising liquid. Look for ribs with a deep marbling pattern and a clean, dry surface; avoid any cut that has been pre-trimmed of its fat cap.

Whiskey Substitutions

Smoked rye is the heart of the glaze, but a straight rye finished over applewood will land in the same neighborhood. In a pinch, a peated single-malt thinned with a teaspoon of molasses approximates the campfire edge. Avoid bourbon here — its sweetness flattens the dish.

Make-Ahead Storage

The ribs improve overnight. Cool the braise uncovered until steam stops rising, then refrigerate the meat submerged in its liquid for up to three days. Lift the solidified fat cap before reheating gently at 300°F. The glaze should be made fresh the day of serving.

Wine Pairing A medium-bodied Syrah from Washington's Columbia Valley — peppery, lightly smoked, with enough acid to cut through the braise — is the natural companion to this plate.
