Fire, Grain
& Memory

An essay by Mireille Aubert, Chef & Proprietor

Long before I understood what a restaurant was, I understood the sound of a kitchen waking up. My grandmother's apartment on the rue Mercière in Lyon began each morning with the percussive ritual of cast iron meeting flame — a kettle, a copper pot, the slow coaxing of a fire that had been banked the night before. She believed that a hearth, properly kept, never truly went out. It only rested. I came to Appalachia thirty-two years later with very little besides knives, a battered copy of La Bonne Cuisine de Madame E. Saint-Ange, and the conviction that the mountains here held something I had been missing in France: a quietness, a generosity, and a wildness that asked to be cooked over open flame.

Maison Cendrée was never meant to be a French restaurant in the literal sense. The building — a converted grist mill from 1887, its timbers still smelling faintly of cornmeal and creek water — refused that narrative the day I walked into it. What it asked for instead was a kind of translation. French technique, yes, but bent toward Carolina chestnut, sorghum, ramps pulled from a hillside in Madison County, trout the color of river stones. Cooking that remembered where it came from while staying entirely awake to where it now stood.

The six courses we serve each evening are, in the strictest sense, an argument. They argue for slowness. They argue for the farmer who drives forty miles to deliver four pounds of celeriac. They argue that fire — real fire, not the suggestion of it — changes a vegetable, a fish, a piece of dry-aged lamb in ways no induction burner has yet replicated.

On Grain

Every loaf of bread on our pass begins six miles upriver, at Cairn Springs Mill, where Lucas and Beatrice Hennig stone-grind heritage wheats — Red Fife, Turkey Red, a soft white called Sonora that nearly went extinct in the 1970s. We buy roughly four hundred pounds a month. We waste almost none of it. The trim from yesterday's milk loaf becomes tomorrow's amuse, the crust scorched against embers and dressed with cultured cream.

We do not so much choose our grain as inherit it — every kernel carries a century of someone's stubbornness, and our only job is to not betray that work in the last six feet of its journey. — On the partnership with Cairn Springs Mill

I am often asked why we do not simply buy flour. The answer is that flour, in the industrial sense, is a kind of forgetting. Whole-grain milling, done weekly and used within days, is the opposite — a deliberate act of remembering. The bread tastes different in October than it does in May, and our guests, if they are paying attention, can taste the difference too.

On Fire

At the center of the kitchen sits a hearth we built over the course of fourteen months from millstones salvaged from the building's original grinding floor. Three of them — granite, quartzite, and a single slab of weathered limestone — form the firebox itself. Oak and hickory burn down through the day; by service, what remains is a bed of embers fine enough to caramelize a leek without scorching its outer leaf.

The hearth was not designed; it was remembered. We laid each stone where it asked to lie, and the fire, when we finally lit it, behaved as though it had been waiting for us. — On the millstone hearth, completed spring 2021

To cook with wood is to accept a degree of unpredictability that modern kitchens have spent a century trying to eliminate. We have made peace with it. Some nights the fire runs hot and the trout sears in ninety seconds. Other nights it sulks, and the pace of service bends, gently, around it. Our guests, almost without exception, seem to find this honest rather than inconvenient.

Five Principles

If a manifesto is what one writes when one wishes to be held accountable, then these are ours — printed each January, posted in the kitchen, and reviewed by the whole team at the start of every season:

  1. Cook with fire, or do not cook
  2. Know the farmer's first name
  3. Waste nothing that grew
  4. Let the season decide the menu
  5. Serve the guest, not the review
